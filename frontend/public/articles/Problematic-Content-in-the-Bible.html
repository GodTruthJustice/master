Problematic Content in the Bible: Ethical Issues, Interpretation, and Debate

 

Introduction

 

The Bible, encompassing the Old and New Testaments, contains numerous passages that modern readers find ethically problematic. These include verses that appear to condone slavery and other human rights violations, impose harsh restrictions on personal freedom, conflict with contemporary universal values of equality and justice, advocate severe treatment of women and children, or promote violence in the name of religion. For example, the Hebrew Bible’s legal codes permit slave-owning, mandate capital punishment for religious offenses, and prescribe patriarchal rules for women’s behavior. Likewise, biblical narratives describe divinely sanctioned wars of extermination and harsh punishments that seem to violate modern standards of human rights. This report provides an analysis of these problematic contents: first summarizing the main areas of concern (slavery, violence, treatment of women, treatment of children, and related issues), then examining whether the biblical text presents these as negatives to learn from or as acceptable norms. Next, it explores how Believer interpreters—both traditional (e.g. Catholic and conservative Protestant) and modern liberal theologians—have defended or rationalized such passages. Finally, it offers counterarguments challenging those defenses. Throughout, we reference theological and historical scholarship to provide context and evidence for each point.

 

Categories of Problematic Content in Scripture

 

Slavery and Human Rights Violations

 

One of the most glaring issues is the Bible’s treatment of slavery. Both Old and New Testament passages regulate and accept slavery as an institution rather than condemn it. In the Old Testament Law of Moses, Israelites were explicitly allowed to purchase slaves from foreign nations and keep them as property permanently. Leviticus 25, for instance, permits taking slaves from neighboring peoples and treating them as inheritable property (Lev 25:44–46). Hebrew slaves (i.e. fellow Israelites) could be kept for six years and freed in the seventh, but non-Hebrew slaves and war captives could be enslaved for life. The laws even state that a master would not be punished if he beat his slave to death as long as the slave survived a day or two, since “the slave is the owner’s property” (Exodus 21:21) . Such passages leave little doubt that chattel slavery (owning another person as property) is affirmed in the Old Testament. Owning many slaves is even portrayed as a sign of divine favor in some narratives (e.g. God blessed patriarchs like Abraham with servants/slaves). There are literally dozens of references to slavery in the Bible mentioned “in passing, without comment” as a normal part of life. Nowhere in the Hebrew Bible are the ethics of slave-owning directly criticized; instead, the text provides rules on how to manage slavery (for example, Exodus 21, Deuteronomy 15). This stands in stark conflict with modern universal human rights, which hold that owning a human being as property is fundamentally unjust.

 

The New Testament also fails to repudiate slavery. On the contrary, several New Testament letters instruct slaves to obey their masters “in everything” (see Ephesians 6:5, Colossians 3:22). The Apostle Paul, while teaching that spiritually “there is neither slave nor free” in Christ (Galatians 3:28), nevertheless advises Believer slaves to submit to their earthly masters with respect and sincerity, and he tells masters to treat slaves kindly but does not tell them to release their slaves (Ephesians 6:5–9, Colossians 4:1). First Peter 2:18 even urges slaves to accept suffering, “even if it means being beaten,” as a commendable example of Christlike endurance. Perhaps most striking is the brief New Testament book of Philemon, a letter from Paul regarding a runaway slave, Onesimus. In this letter, Paul sends Onesimus back to his Believer master Philemon and stops short of demanding that Philemon free him. Paul appeals to Philemon to receive Onesimus “no longer as a slave, but better than a slave, as a dear brother” (Philemon 1:16) and hints that Philemon should do “even more than I say” (1:21), which many interpret as a suggestion of freeing him. Yet Paul does not explicitly condemn slaveholding; he couches his request politely and within the existing system of slavery. As a result, slaveholders throughout history found plenty of biblical support for their practices. Indeed, during the 19th-century debates on slavery in America, Southern slave owners frequently “waved Bibles over their heads” and pointed out that nowhere does the Bible say “Believers can’t own people” . Critics note that if the Bible were truly the literal and infallible word of a just God, it is troubling that it “got the easiest moral question humanity has ever faced wrong” by failing to clearly forbid slavery. The acceptance of slavery in Scripture represents a profound conflict with today’s values of freedom and human dignity.

 

Violence, Warfare, and Religious Intolerance

 

Another major category of problematic content is the extreme violence commanded or carried out in the Bible, often with divine approval. The Old Testament, in particular, contains many accounts of warfare, massacre, and even genocide. For example, in the conquest of Canaan under Joshua, the Israelites are repeatedly instructed by God to exterminate entire populations of cities: “you shall utterly destroy them… show them no mercy” (Deuteronomy 7:2, 20:16-17). In the Book of Joshua, this command is carried out at cities like Jericho, where “men and women, young and old, oxen, sheep, and donkeys” were put to the sword . Similarly, in 1 Samuel 15:3, God instructs King Saul to attack the Amalekites and “totally destroy all that belongs to them. Do not spare them; put to death men and women, children and infants.” These texts depict God ordering what we would now label genocide – the killing of entire ethnic groups, including non-combatants and children. In the biblical narrative, such actions are presented as a form of divine judgment or as necessary for the protection of Israel from idolatry. There is no indication within the stories that this violence is morally problematic; on the contrary, failure to fully carry out the ban (herem) is rebuked (as when Saul is rejected by God for sparing some Amalekites and livestock in 1 Samuel 15). These accounts raise severe ethical concerns: How can the mass killing of infants and innocents be justified? Modern readers often feel that these stories conflict sharply with universal humanitarian values.

 

Beyond warfare, religious intolerance and violent punishment for dissent are enshrined in biblical law. The First Commandment forbids worship of other gods, and laws in Deuteronomy prescribe the death penalty for idolatry or apostasy. Deuteronomy 13 famously mandates that if anyone, even a family member, entices you to serve other gods, “you shall not yield to them or listen to them… You shall stone them to death” – “your hand shall be first against them to put them to death”, and “you shall not pity them” . Entire towns that turn to other gods are to be destroyed with the sword, their inhabitants and even livestock slaughtered and the town burned to rubble (Deut. 13:12-18). In essence, the Bible’s law codes envision a theocratic society with zero tolerance for religious divergence, enforced by capital punishment. Such prescriptions violate modern principles of freedom of religion and belief. Similarly, blasphemy was punishable by death (Leviticus 24:16). Adultery and certain sexual behaviors were also capital crimes under the Torah. The cumulative impression is a legal system that employs execution as punishment for a wide range of offenses, including things that today would be considered matters of personal conscience or private morality. This draconian approach is at odds with contemporary views on human rights and the limits of state (or religious) violence.

 

Even God’s own actions in the Bible can be seen as disturbingly violent. The Genesis Flood (Genesis 6–9) describes God wiping out virtually all of humanity (and land animals) with a cataclysmic flood, sparing only Noah’s family—essentially an act of mass destruction of life on earth presented as righteous judgment for human wickedness. In the story of Sodom and Gomorrah (Genesis 19), God destroys entire cities with fire and brimstone. The Plagues of Egypt climax with the death of every firstborn child in Egypt (Exodus 12), a divinely inflicted mass killing of children to force Pharaoh’s hand. These narratives portray God directly causing widespread death and suffering. In the New Testament, while the overall tone is less focused on earthly violence, there are passages that remain problematic. The Book of Revelation contains graphically violent imagery of divine wrath (plagues, wars, and a final battle) and the establishment of God’s kingdom by force. Jesus himself, though preaching love and enemy-forgiveness in many places, also speaks of coming judgment using violent metaphors (e.g. in a parable he describes a king saying “as for those enemies of mine who did not want me to reign over them—bring them here and slaughter them in my presence”, Luke 19:27). Such verses have at times been taken out of context to justify religious violence. At minimum, they show that even the New Testament is not entirely free of violent content (for instance, Jesus predicting he brings “not peace but a sword” in Matthew 10:34, which, while generally interpreted as metaphorical division, can be unsettling to readers).

 

Crucially, many of these violent passages are not explicitly presented as bad examples. Instead, when violence is commanded by God or carried out by Israel at God’s behest, the text implies approval. The conquest of Canaan is narrated as the fulfillment of God’s promise and as a holy duty; there is no voice within the text questioning the ethics of the slaughter. When the prophets later recall these events, they do not condemn Joshua for violence; rather, disobedience to God’s command (like failing to eliminate all idolaters) is criticized. This lack of internal critique makes these passages especially challenging. Some other violent stories do have a negative framing – for example, the Book of Judges depicts a time of chaos and moral degeneracy, and it includes horrific violence (like the rape and murder of the Levite’s concubine in Judges 19, or Jephthah’s sacrifice of his daughter in Judges 11). These stories are meant to illustrate how bad things got “when everyone did what was right in their own eyes” (Judges 21:25) in the absence of righteous leadership . Feminist scholars point out, however, that even in such cases the text often does not explicitly condemn the violence against women; the cruelty is described, but no divine law is given to punish the perpetrators in the narrative . This leads some critics to argue that the Bible gives “tacit approval” to these acts by not speaking out against them . In summary, the Bible’s handling of violence—frequently encouraging or commanding it in certain contexts—conflicts with modern ideals of peace, human rights, and the intrinsic value of human life (including the lives of enemies or non-believers).

 

Treatment of Women and Gender Inequality

 

The Bible was composed in patriarchal cultures, and this context is reflected in many passages that modern readers see as sexist or oppressive toward women. In the Old Testament, women’s social and legal status was generally subordinate to men. Women were often viewed as the property or dependents of their fathers or husbands. For instance, the Ten Commandments list a man’s wife among his possessions not to covet (Exodus 20:17), implying wives were part of a man’s household property. Various laws in the Torah illustrate inequality: a daughter could be given in marriage without her consent, polygamy was practiced by many prominent men (and regulated in law rather than forbidden), and men could take concubines (slave-wives) with fewer rights. If a woman was raped, the law’s solution in certain cases was that she had to marry her rapist: Deuteronomy 22:28–29 states that a man who rapes an unbetrothed virgin must pay the girl’s father a bride-price and “she shall become his wife.” This was presumably meant to ensure the woman’s financial security after the assault (since her prospects for marriage were damaged) , but from a modern perspective it is a horrific prospect – effectively a law forcing a victim to live with her attacker. (Some apologists argue the woman’s consent was still required or that it was primarily an obligation on the man, but the text itself does not voice the woman’s perspective.) Adultery laws also treated women differently: a married woman who slept with a man was committing adultery (a crime punishable by death for both parties), but a married man who slept with an unmarried woman was not necessarily considered an adulterer (polygyny was allowed). Inheritances typically passed through male lines; daughters inherited only if there were no sons (Numbers 27). Women could be divorced by husbands relatively easily in ancient Israel (Deut. 24:1-4) whereas a wife did not have a similar right. Moreover, certain purity laws placed additional burdens on women – for example, after giving birth, a woman was considered ritually “unclean” for 40 days if she bore a son, but 80 days if she bore a daughter (Leviticus 12:1-5), a distinction that many see as implying a lesser status for female offspring. All these reflect cultural norms of the ancient Near East, but they conflict with modern principles of gender equality and women’s rights.

 

Narrative sections of the Bible also contain disturbing treatment of women. Women are sometimes subjected to sexual violence and their suffering is narrated with little comment. Two of the most “egregious examples” of violence against women in Scripture occur in Judges: Jephthah’s daughter (Judges 11:29–40), whom Jephthah tragically sacrifices due to a rash vow, and the unnamed concubine of a Levite who is gang-raped and left to die (Judges 19) . These texts are gut-wrenching. While the overall context of Judges implies Israel’s moral breakdown, the stories themselves do not include editorial remarks explicitly condemning the acts. The horror is apparent, but the Bible often “speaks in silence” – it presents the deed and its consequences (e.g. a civil war erupting after the outrage in Judges 19–20) rather than direct moral commentary. This ambiguity has led to debate: some argue the Bible is simply describing human sin here (not endorsing it), yet others point out that God never intervenes or provides justice for the female victims in these stories, raising questions about the text’s stance . Elsewhere, the Bible’s heroes themselves treat women in ways that clash with modern ethics. Lot, for instance, in Genesis 19 offers his virgin daughters to an angry mob to protect his male guests – an act portrayed as misguided hospitality at best, and deeply immoral by today’s standards. Yet Lot is not explicitly condemned in the text for this offer; instead, he is later called “righteous” in the New Testament (2 Peter 2:7), likely for opposing Sodom’s sins, not for his treatment of his daughters.

 

In the New Testament, women’s status improves in some respects (Jesus’s inclusive attitude toward women, female disciples like Mary Magdalene, etc.), but certain passages remain problematic. Paul’s epistles contain instructions that women should be submissive and silent in church gatherings and that they are not permitted to teach or have authority over men. The clearest example is 1 Corinthians 14:34-35: “As in all the churches of the saints, the women should keep silence in the churches. For they are not permitted to speak, but should be in submission, as the law says. If there is anything they desire to know, let them ask their husbands at home. For it is shameful for a woman to speak in church.” Similarly, 1 Timothy 2:11-12 (a letter attributed to Paul, though likely written by a later follower) says a woman must learn in quietness and full submission and “I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man.” These passages have been used for centuries to bar women from leadership roles in church and to justify a general doctrine of female subordination in both church and home. They are in tension with other New Testament evidence of women playing active roles in the early Believer movement (for example, Priscilla teaching Apollos in Acts 18, or Phoebe being a deacon commended by Paul in Romans 16). Nonetheless, the plain words of the text present a restrictive view of women’s role. Likewise, the household codes in Ephesians and Colossians instruct, “Wives, submit to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord” (Col. 3:18, Eph. 5:22) and only then tell husbands to love their wives. The authority hierarchy (husband as head, wife as subordinate) is taken for granted. First Peter 3:1 says similarly, “wives, be subject to your own husbands,” and even if the husband is an unbeliever, her reverent behavior might win him over. Peter also calls women “the weaker vessel” (1 Pet. 3:7) while urging husbands to honor them.

 

Such texts clearly reflect a worldview in which gender roles are unequal – men lead, women follow. This conflicts with modern assertions of women’s equal capability and rights in all spheres of life. To many today, verses about silencing women or compelling their submission are not just antiquated, but unjust. Even if these instructions made sense in a first-century context, taken as timeless divine commands they have upheld systems that limit women’s freedom (for example, some Believer traditions long forbade women from higher education or jobs, citing these scriptures). Notably, the Bible contains no outright statement that women possess equal authority to men in family or church governance. At best, one can find a principle of spiritual equality (“in Christ there is no male or female,” Gal. 3:28) and examples of women whom God used in significant ways (Deborah as a judge and prophetess, for instance). But those exceptions do not overturn the general patriarchal pattern in Scripture. No biblical author systematically argues for women’s social equality as we understand it today. Therefore, many passages about women come into direct conflict with contemporary values of gender equality, women’s rights, and personal autonomy. They can be deeply troubling, especially to women readers who find that the holy text seems to relegate them to silence or second-class status.

 

Harsh Punishments and Children’s Rights

 

The Bible also contains commands and narratives that modern readers see as violating children’s rights and welfare. A stark example is the law of the “rebellious son” in Deuteronomy 21:18-21. This law states that if parents have a stubborn and disobedient son who refuses to reform, they can bring him to the city elders and have him stoned to death by the men of the city. “All the men of his city shall stone him with stones, so that he dies. So you shall purge the evil from your midst, and all Israel shall hear, and fear” (Deut. 21:21). There is no age explicitly given, but the context implies an older adolescent (he is called “son” but is a glutton and drunkard, indicating at least a teenager). While some later Jewish interpreters greatly limited this law’s applicability (arguing, for example, that it was never actually carried out and was meant as a theoretical deterrent) , in the text itself this punishment is presented as a legitimate option to “purge evil.” It is effectively a death penalty for juvenile delinquency. To any modern sensibility, executing a child for being rebellious is an appalling notion and a gross violation of a child’s basic rights to life and due process. There is no indication in Scripture that this law was to be taken as symbolic; it appears in the legal code alongside other rules. As noted, later Jewish tradition records that “there never had been, nor ever will be, a rebellious son [executed]; the law was given for educational purposes” . While this mitigates the practical horror, it underscores that the Torah’s literal words, if applied, would allow such an execution. The existence of such a law in the Bible is hard to reconcile with modern norms that children should be protected and guided with patience, not subject to violent retribution.

 

More commonly cited are the proverbs advocating corporal punishment of children. Proverbs 13:24 declares, “Whoever spares the rod hates his son, but he who loves him is diligent to discipline him.” Similarly, Proverbs 23:13-14 says one should not withhold discipline: “If you strike him with the rod, he will not die. If you strike him with the rod, you will save his soul from Sheol.” These verses have been summarized in the saying “spare the rod, spoil the child.” Traditional readers take them as endorsement of physical spanking or whipping as a necessary form of discipline. Indeed, such verses have been widely cited in many cultures to justify corporal punishment of children in the home and even in schools . In recent decades, however, a growing understanding of children’s psychological and physical well-being has led many to view corporal punishment as harmful “abuse” rather than loving discipline. Child welfare advocates strongly criticize any suggestion that beating children is divinely approved. The Bible’s advice on using the “rod” is therefore seen by some as promoting violence against children. Apologists counter that these proverbs intend disciplined guidance, not abuse, and should be understood in the context of loving correction . Nonetheless, the plain imagery of a rod and the statement that not using it means you hate your child strikes many today as contrary to the principle that child-rearing should avoid physical harm and prioritize positive reinforcement. The Bible includes no explicit acknowledgment of children’s autonomous rights or personhood separate from their parents’ authority. Children are generally viewed as under the dominion of parents, who even have power of life and death (as seen in the rebellious son law). This, too, is inconsistent with modern conceptions of children’s rights.

 

Beyond disciplinary issues, some biblical narratives involve children in episodes of violence and suffering that raise moral questions. We have already mentioned how in wartime narratives, even infants are not spared (as in the Amalekite and Canaanite wars). The story of Abraham’s near-sacrifice of Isaac (Genesis 22) is often praised within the Judeo-Believer tradition as the ultimate test of faith and obedience to God. Yet, ethically, the idea that God would command a father to slit his own child’s throat as a sacrifice is deeply troubling (even though, in the story, God stops Abraham at the last moment). The narrative’s focus is on Abraham’s faith, not on Isaac’s trauma. Some readers feel this story borders on endorsing the notion that blind obedience to God transcends even the most basic parental instinct to protect one’s child. In a modern view, asking a parent to kill their child is an unconscionable demand, not a virtuous test. Likewise, the story of Jephthah’s daughter (Judges 11) features a father sacrificing his only daughter due to a vow—while the text portrays it as tragic and foolish, it does not explicitly say God disapproved (and Jephthah is listed among people of faith in Hebrews 11:32). Instances like these, where children are victimized (with Jephthah’s daughter essentially a human sacrifice) or put at risk in religious contexts, are hard to reconcile with the inherent value of children’s lives recognized today.

 

Finally, an especially grim verse often cited is Psalm 137:9, where the psalmist, exiled in Babylon, expresses rage against Israel’s enemies and says, “Happy is he who seizes your infants and dashes them against the rocks.” This shocking image of infanticide is a cry for vengeance. Though it is poetic hyperbole spoken by a human (not a command from God), its presence in the Bible is jolting. It exemplifies the raw violence in some biblical poetry that conflicts with modern ethics. Readers understand the context of suffering behind such a line, yet it remains an example of violent rhetoric that is hard to square with universal love of neighbor.

 

In summary, concerning women and children, the Bible contains numerous elements—from patriarchal laws and misogynistic comments to draconian punishments and violent imagery involving the young—that clash with today’s ideals of equality, compassion, and protection for the vulnerable. The key question becomes: Does the Bible endorse these problematic ideas, or are they simply part of the narrative backdrop to be understood but not emulated? We turn next to examine how these stories are presented within Scripture’s own moral framework.

 

Negative Examples or Endorsed Norms? (Biblical Perspective)

 

It’s important to analyze whether each problematic story or law in the Bible is presented as something negative (to teach a lesson against it) or as an endorsed norm. The Bible is an anthology of texts spanning many centuries, and it contains both descriptive passages (narratives of what happened, not necessarily condoning it) and prescriptive passages (commands or laws). Understanding which is which can influence how we judge the text’s stance on these issues.

 

In some cases, the Bible itself makes clear that a story of wrongdoing is meant to be a cautionary tale. A good example is the violence and chaos in the Book of Judges. The final chapters of Judges (17–21) contain horrific events like civil war and the abuse of the Levite’s concubine. These are bracketed by the comment, “In those days there was no king in Israel; everyone did what was right in his own eyes” (Judg. 21:25) . This refrain signals that the narrator views the situation as disordered and negative. Scholars note that the “deteriorating status of women and the violence done to them” in Judges is depicted as a barometer of Israel’s moral decline . In other words, the Bible is not celebrating the rape and murder in Judges 19; rather, it’s showing how far the people fell from God’s ideal. Similarly, King David’s adultery with Bathsheba and his arranged murder of her husband (2 Samuel 11) are clearly portrayed as sins—David is confronted by the prophet Nathan and punished by God (2 Sam. 12). Thus, narratives of individual moral failures (like David’s sin, or Solomon’s polygamy leading him into idolatry) are often explicitly or implicitly negative examples. We are meant to learn “don’t do this; it leads to disaster.”

 

On the other hand, many troubling passages are not presented as wrongful at all, but rather as righteous or divinely authorized. This is especially true for divine commands and legal stipulations. For instance, the laws on slavery in Exodus and Leviticus are given in the same matter-of-fact tone as laws on theft or restitution; there is no suggestion in the text that slavery is regrettable. To the contrary, these rules are part of God’s covenant code for Israel. This implies that the biblical authors (and presumably their understanding of God’s will) considered regulated slavery morally acceptable at that time. No prophet ever rises up in Israel to say “Thus says the Lord: Free all your slaves.” The abolition of slavery is not a theme found anywhere in Scripture; the abolitionist impulse arose much later in history (with Believers eventually opposing slavery in spite of the Bible’s clear permission of it, rather than because of an explicit biblical mandate). Therefore, one must conclude that within the biblical world, slavery was normalized, not presented as an evil to eliminate. The oppression of the Israelites as slaves in Egypt is portrayed as a great evil (Exodus portrays Egyptian slavery as cruel and unjust), but notably, once free, the Israelites are allowed to enslave others. The Bible thus condemns slavery only when Israelites are the victims, not the practice per se. That nuance suggests an ethnocentric morality (what’s wrong is oppressing God’s chosen people; oppressing others is not equally denounced in the text). So those passages are not cautionary tales; they are rules apparently endorsed by God for ancient society.

 

Similarly, the commands to destroy the Canaanites are given as God’s direct instructions to Moses and Joshua. There is no indication that this genocide is meant to be seen as anything but necessary and good in its context (the text justifies it by the wickedness of those nations and the danger of idolatry, e.g. Deut. 20:17-18). The Israelites who carry it out (Joshua, etc.) are praised for obedience, whereas failures to fully annihilate are criticized. Thus, such violence is presented as virtuous obedience, not as immoral. Another indicator is that later generations looked back on Joshua’s conquest positively – the book of Psalms, for example, praises God’s faithfulness in helping Israel defeat nations and gain their land (Psalm 105, 106). There is no subsequent biblical critique of the morality of the conquest. This means the promotion of violence in those texts is indeed part of the Bible’s endorsed narrative, not a deviation that is corrected within Scripture. The same goes for laws like executing idolaters or Sabbath-breakers: those laws are given by God through Moses; the narrative in Numbers 15:32-36 where a man is stoned to death for gathering sticks on the Sabbath shows God Himself commanding the community to carry out the execution. The text offers no apology for this severity; it states it as justice according to the law. So, from an internal standpoint, strict theocratic punishments are upheld as righteous, not as mistakes.

 

When it comes to women’s roles, the prescriptive texts (like Paul’s directives for silence or submission) are presented as proper order in the church, not as examples of bias to later overcome. There is no internal New Testament voice saying “actually, women can speak/lead just as men do.” Any such egalitarian perspective had to be read between the lines or argued from broader principles. The explicit instructions remain restrictive. Only much later, through historical development, did most churches re-evaluate these instructions. But within the biblical canon itself, verses limiting women are not framed as temporary or problematic; they’re given as the Apostle’s command “as in all churches”. This makes them particularly challenging: they sound like universal principles, which is why they held such sway in church practice for so long.

 

In other narratives, the viewpoint is less clear, and readers debate the intent. For example, Jephthah’s sacrifice of his daughter: the story (Judges 11) certainly paints the event as tragic and foolish—Jephthah is not rewarded; his line ends. It likely is meant to warn against rash vows. However, the text doesn’t explicitly say “and Jephthah did evil in God’s eyes,” leaving some ambiguity. But given the broader biblical condemnation of human sacrifice elsewhere, one can reasonably infer that this was not endorsed by God (indeed, Jephthah is an example of faith in Hebrews 11 for his trust in God during battle, not for the vow). Lot’s offer of his daughters in Sodom is another morally repugnant act that is not explicitly commented on; there the text’s silence might imply it was obviously abhorrent (the story as a whole shows the depravity of the situation). Polygamy is an interesting case: many patriarchs and kings had multiple wives, and the narratives do not overtly condemn them for that. Yet they often show negative consequences (jealousy, conflict, etc.), and by the New Testament period, monogamy is upheld as the ideal for church leaders. So some argue the Bible subtly teaches the problems of polygamy through narrative outcomes, even if it never bans it outright in the Old Testament. In general, though, where the Bible wanted to make a moral lesson clear, it usually did so either through direct command or prophetic critique. The absence of such critique for slavery, holy war, patriarchal authority, etc., strongly suggests that the biblical authors did not view these as problematic in their time.

 

To summarize: some problematic stories are indeed depicted as negative examples of human sin (e.g. the rape in Judges is part of illustrating Israel’s corruption, David’s abuse of power is punished, etc.), but many problematic laws and commands are portrayed as divine ideals or necessary actions, not as evils. This mixture complicates interpretation. It means readers cannot simply dismiss all troubling passages as “the Bible showing what not to do.” In many cases the Bible seems to be telling people to do precisely those troubling things (kill idolaters, own slaves, etc.). This tension has led to extensive efforts by believers to explain or justify these passages, which we will examine next.

 

Believer Interpretations and Defenses of Troubling Passages

 

Faced with these morally challenging scriptures, Believers throughout history have developed various methods to defend, rationalize, or reinterpret them. Broadly speaking, responses fall into two categories: traditional approaches, which uphold the divine authority of the Bible but explain the passages in contextual or theological ways; and modern liberal approaches, which may question the inerrancy of such texts or interpret them non-literally to align with ethical principles. We will survey both, including perspectives from Catholic and Protestant traditions, and then outline possible counterarguments.

 

Traditional Believer Defenses (Catholic and Conservative Protestant)

 

Traditional Believer theology maintains that the Bible is inspired by God and fundamentally truthful in what it teaches. Therefore, it cannot simply be wrong or immoral. When confronted with passages about slavery, genocide, or subjugation of women, traditional interpreters seek to harmonize these with God’s goodness. Several common strategies are used:

·       Appeal to Context and Progressive Revelation: A frequent explanation is that God’s moral revelation was gradual, meeting people where they were in their historical context and leading them step by step to higher moral understanding. This idea of “divine pedagogy” or progressive revelation is emphasized in Catholic teaching. Pope Benedict XVI wrote that one must understand the biblical belief in God by following its historical development, and that God’s plan “is manifested progressively and… suited to the cultural and moral level of distant times.” In other words, the ancient Israelites at 1200 BC could not yet grasp the full ethics that would be revealed through Christ; so God worked within their cultural framework (which included slavery, patriarchal structures, etc.), tolerating certain things for a time while gradually pushing towards improvement . The Catholic Church in Verbum Domini (a document on Scripture) acknowledges that the Bible sometimes narrates events or commands without condemning their immorality by modern standards precisely because of this accommodation to ancient moral development . For example, Catholic commentators view the herem (ban) warfare of the Old Testament as “a survival from more primitive times” that disappeared as Israel’s morality grew more enlightened . By the time of Jesus, God’s fuller revelation of love and mercy had arrived, making such practices obsolete. Thus, a traditional Believer might say: Yes, the Old Testament law allowed slavery and harsh punishments, but God was working gradually—those laws were a starting point, not the end of moral revelation.

·       The Old Covenant vs. the New Covenant: In line with the above, many argue that Christ fulfilled and superseded the Old Testament law. The old law, with its severity, was meant for a particular era and purpose (to govern Israel and expose sin), but Believers are under a new law of love. This goes back to the early Church Fathers; for instance, St. Augustine taught that if an Old Testament law seems to conflict with charity (love), one should understand it spiritually rather than literally for today. The idea is that Jesus’ teachings correct or elevate prior norms. A classic example cited: Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount said, “You have heard it said, ‘An eye for an eye…’ but I say to you, resist not evil; turn the other cheek” – indicating that the older justice law (which itself limited vengeance) is now transcended by a higher ethic of forgiveness. By analogy, harsh measures like the ban on Canaanites might be seen as specific to a time and not applicable after Christ’s revelation. The Catholic perspective indeed states: “Problematic passages in the Old Testament are valid insofar as they are part of the history leading up to Christ.” They are to be read “in light of Christ” – meaning we filter them through what Jesus taught. If something seems contrary to Jesus’ character, we are not to take it as a standalone moral guide but as part of a larger salvation story that culminates in Jesus. Traditionalists will often quote Jesus and the apostles to emphasize the shift: for example, “God is love” (1 John 4:8) becomes the lens through which troubling texts must ultimately be understood (even if that means they were provisional or partial revelations).

·       Hyperbole or Non-Literal Interpretation: Another approach is to claim that some of the most extreme commands were not meant to be taken literally or did not actually happen as written. Apologist Paul Copan and others argue that the language of total destruction in the conquest narratives is Ancient Near Eastern hyperbole – similar to sports talk (“We annihilated our opponents!”) . They note that the Bible itself, after saying “utterly destroy,” sometimes indicates survivors, suggesting those phrases were conventional war rhetoric. Thus, they maintain that God did not intend actual genocide; rather, the command was to defeat the Canaanites militarily and drive them out, not necessarily kill every last woman and child . This view tries to lessen the moral problem by asserting the Bible isn’t describing what we think it is; we’re misreading genre. Similarly, some claim that the law in Deuteronomy 22 about marrying a rape victim has been misunderstood – that it was actually about consensual premarital sex or that it was protective legislation, not forcing the woman but forcing the man to provide for her (the finer points of Hebrew wording are debated to soften the scenario) . In the New Testament context, some conservative scholars even question whether 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 (women’s silence) was an interpolation (a later addition), since it contrasts with other parts of Paul’s letters. While this view (held by many modern scholars) is not mainstream among traditional inerrantists (because it implies a copyist added it), it shows one way to handle a troubling verse: suggest it might not have been original Pauline teaching. Generally, however, the hyperbole and context argument is popular: e.g., slavery in the Bible is often explained as “not the race-based brutal slavery of modern times, but a gentler form of servitude or indentured service”. Apologists note that Israelite slaves had to be freed after 7 years (Exod. 21:2) and that kidnapping someone to sell as a slave was a capital crime (Exod. 21:16). They argue that biblical-era slavery was more like a social safety net for debt and not based on dehumanizing an entire race. They also highlight that masters were exhorted to treat slaves well (Eph. 6:9) and that many early Believers freed slaves. All these points serve to contextualize slavery, implying the Bible’s tolerance of it was a regulated, somewhat improved version of an ancient institution, not an endorsement of the atrocities of 18th–19th century slavery. In short, traditional defenses often try to show the problematic practice was “not as bad as it looks” in context.

·       Divine Sovereignty and Justice: Another classic defense, especially among more conservative Protestants, is the appeal to God’s sovereignty and higher moral understanding. This is essentially: God, as the author of life, has the right to give and take life; His judgments, though severe, are just, even if we humans don’t fully grasp them. In this view, the Canaanite genocide is justified because of the extreme wickedness of those cultures (the Old Testament notes their sins like child sacrifice, incest, etc., e.g. Lev. 18:24-25), and God’s patience had run out after centuries. Thus, the destruction was a unique divine judgment, akin to the Flood, to purge evil. Some argue that killing the children of these nations, while tragic, ensured they would not grow up in that evil environment – and some even speculate in an attempt to be theologically consistent that those children might be saved in the afterlife by God’s mercy. Apologist William Lane Craig has infamously argued that the children who died went to heaven, so in a sense God’s command, while hard, was ultimately a mercy to them compared to growing up in depravity. This kind of argument takes a divine command theory approach: if God commanded it, it must be morally right by definition, because God is the source of morality. Similarly, regarding slavery or harsh punishments, one might say: God allowed those for a time for His purposes; who are we, as fallible humans, to judge God’s directives? This defense leans on faith in God’s goodness and the assumption that our moral intuitions are not superior to God’s revelation. Passages like “Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?” (Gen. 18:25) or God’s speech to Job (“Where were you when I laid the earth’s foundation?” Job 38:4) are cited to remind believers that God’s ways may be beyond human understanding. In effect, this is an appeal to humility: even if a command seems immoral to us, if it comes from God in Scripture, we trust there is a righteous reason behind it (perhaps as a stern lesson about the seriousness of sin, or the need for purity in Israel to eventually bring forth the Messiah, etc.).

·       Allegorical or Spiritual Interpretation: Particularly in older Believer tradition (like some Church Fathers and later theological readings), troubling texts were often interpreted allegorically rather than literally. For instance, the genocidal wars were re-read as metaphors for spiritual warfare – the eradication of sin from one’s life. The famous early Believer scholar Origen taught that when Joshua battles the Canaanites, it can symbolize the soul’s battle against vices; thus God’s command to “destroy them utterly” is applied to how ruthlessly we should root out sin, not to actual human violence. This approach allowed Believers to derive edifying meaning without endorsing the surface brutality. Likewise, the erotic and polygamous aspects of the patriarchs’ lives could be allegorized (e.g., the two wives of Jacob, Leah and Rachel, were seen as symbols of different covenants, rather than models for marital practice). The Catholic tradition has long allowed for non-literal “spiritual senses” of Scripture, meaning that even if the literal sense is harsh, the underlying spiritual lesson might be different and more uplifting. This is a way of sidestepping the ethical issue by saying the text’s ultimate purpose isn’t to teach ethics in that instance but to prefigure some spiritual truth. For example, one Catholic Answers resource notes that interpreting difficult Old Testament violence requires expertise and reading “within the Believer perspective, which has as its ultimate key the gospel” – in practice, this means seeing those events as part of salvation history or allegory, not as moral directives to imitate.

·       Highlighting Countervailing Biblical Principles: Another softer defense is to stress that the Bible also contains the seeds of the very moral truths that later abolished slavery and improved women’s status. For instance, Believers often point out that Genesis says humans (male and female) are made in God’s image, implying equal value (Genesis 1:27). They cite Jesus’s unusually respectful treatment of women and outcasts as evidence that the trajectory of the Bible is toward dignity and compassion. They also note that the New Testament taught love of neighbor and even love of enemy as supreme commandments, which, if fully realized, would negate the mistreatment of others. Saint Paul’s little letter to Philemon is often leveraged: Paul doesn’t explicitly command Philemon to free Onesimus, but he strongly hints that Philemon should treat this slave as a beloved brother in Christ. “Receive him as you would receive me,” Paul writes (Philem. 1:17) and speaks of Onesimus now being “more than a slave, a brother.” Apologists interpret this as Paul sowing the seeds of abolition, effectively telling a Believer slaveowner that the Believer thing to do is manumit (free) the slave. Similarly, Galatians 3:28’s statement “there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus” is frequently cited as the grand principle that outweighs the culturally specific regulations. Traditional defenders argue that Christianity ultimately undermined slavery and elevated the status of women over time because of these core teachings, even if the earliest Believers did not directly overturn social institutions. They credit the Bible with a revolutionary concept of human equality before God that eventually, under the influence of the gospel, made practices like slavery untenable. This view portrays problematic verses as misunderstood or secondary in light of the overarching biblical message of love, justice, and the equal worth of every person. For example, one Believer blogger claims that “the deeper you go into the Bible’s view of women, the more clear it becomes that… the Bible undermines the oppression of women at every turn.” While a surface reading finds regressive rules, these apologists contend that in context the Bible was actually progressive for its time and set principles that led to greater freedom later. In essence, they say: Don’t just read the isolated harsh passages; see the big picture of redemption and improvement.

 

All of these approaches—contextualizing historically, dividing old covenant from new, reading difficult texts metaphorically, trusting God’s higher reasons, and emphasizing the Bible’s positive values—are employed by traditional Believer teachers to explain why the problematic passages do not actually impugn the Bible’s goodness or God’s character. For example, the Catholic Church teaches that one “cannot simply mechanically apply” every sentence of the Bible today as a moral absolute ; one must discern the intent in context. Catholic apologists explicitly acknowledge that the Old Testament authors “were not privy to the fullness of divine revelation” and may have genuinely thought God wanted certain violent acts, even if God in His providence was accommodating their mindset . They maintain that for doctrine and salvation, the Bible is without error, but this allows that some commandments were temporary or culturally bound. Meanwhile, many evangelical Protestant apologists hold that the Bible, correctly understood, does not endorse anything unjust—so if it seems to, we must be misinterpreting or missing context. For instance, an evangelical writer, after examining the conquest narratives, might conclude: “God did command the Canaanite war, but it was a unique, unrepeatable act of judgment in salvation history, morally justified by the circumstances and by God’s rights as Judge.” Simultaneously, another evangelical (like Greg Boyd, crossing into a more liberal stance) might say: “No, God could not have literally commanded genocide; those texts reflect the human author’s fallen perspective.” Yet even Boyd doesn’t simply throw the text away; he tries to see the crucified Christ as the lens, suggesting that the ugliness of such texts reveals the ugliness of human sin that Christ bore. In sum, traditional approaches either justify the passages within a larger divine plan or reinterpret them in less offensive ways. The goal is to uphold the Bible as not fundamentally immoral. The most common refrain is that the Bible must be read in context – historical, cultural, and canonical (i.e. in the context of the whole Bible). As one Believer source advises, “When you come to a problematic passage… remain humble, reserve judgment, and get to know the land” (meaning the culture of the time) . This humility before the text is urged so that one might find an explanation that resolves the moral tension.

 

Modern Liberal Theological Approaches

 

Modern liberal Believers often take a different tack: they may question the traditional view of the Bible’s perfection or accept that some biblical commands and narratives are simply wrong by today’s standards (since they were a product of their time). Liberal theology tends to emphasize the spirit over the letter, and the idea of an ongoing revelation or ethical discernment guided by reason, experience, and the core gospel values. Here are some key points in liberal Believer responses:

·       Acknowledging Human Influence and Error: Liberal Believers are generally more willing to say that the Bible, while inspired, is also a very human document, reflecting the imperfections and limited understandings of its authors. They might affirm that God’s character is loving and just, but the biblical writers at times depicted God as sanctioning things that, in truth, God would not endorse. For example, theologian Gregory Boyd argues that when Israel attributed violence to God, it was because of their “fallen, violence-prone minds” assuming God must be like other warrior deities . This doesn’t mean the text has no value – it might reveal how God accommodates human weakness or it might carry a deeper message – but it means not every command recorded is a direct reflection of God’s heart. A liberal Believer might straightforwardly say: the slavery laws in the Old Testament, though in Scripture, do not represent the ultimate ethic God desires; they represent what ancient people thought was acceptable. Similarly, some assert that the command to stone gays (Lev. 20:13) or apostates was never something God wanted; rather it was the ancient community’s way of enforcing identity, included in their scriptures. This approach frankly admits there are “errors and harmful teachings” in the Bible due to the “ignorant, superstitious, and cruel age” in which it was written . This echoes humanist critiques but coming from believers who nonetheless cherish the Bible’s higher truths. Essentially, liberal Believers might separate the timeless message (e.g. love, justice, mercy) from the time-bound baggage. They rely on moral intuition and core biblical principles to judge those parts of scripture. If a passage contradicts the revelation of love in Jesus, they feel justified in repudiating that passage’s face value.

·       Reinterpretation Through Jesus and Love: Many liberal Believers take “reading in the light of Jesus” even further than Catholics, to effectively filter out or transform problematic texts. For instance, they might point to Jesus in the Gospels correcting the Old Law: “You have heard it said, but I say to you…” as precedent that not all Old Testament regulations are final. Jesus prioritized love, mercy, and justice – “the weightier matters of the law” (Matthew 23:23) – and said all the law and prophets hang on the commands to love God and neighbor. Therefore, anything in Scripture that seems to violate the law of love must be re-read or set aside. A stark example: some liberal Believers view the genocidal narratives as incompatible with the God revealed in Jesus. They may conclude those stories are theologically flawed portrayals – perhaps Israel believed God told them to do that, but in reality, they were wrong. This is admittedly a bold stance, as it challenges traditional doctrines of biblical inerrancy. Yet, increasing numbers of Believers (especially in mainline Protestant and progressive evangelical circles) are embracing a more critical approach. They might say: The Bible contains God’s word, but also contains human words that we must discern and sometimes disagree with. This is akin to how one might treat church history – there are holy inspirations and also regrettable errors. Progressive Believer writers often encourage “truth-telling” about the Bible, not glossing over the tough parts. One such writer frankly states that it’s troublesome when “truth-telling” the Bible’s story involves describing “a God being violent and commanding humans to be violent”, and criticizes the tendency to either explain it away or ignore it in the church . Instead, they advocate openly wrestling with these texts, possibly concluding they reflect ancient people’s perceptions rather than God’s ideal will.

·       Selective Authority (Canon within the Canon): In practice, many modern Believers operate with a “canon within the canon” – elevating certain scriptures (like the teachings of Jesus, the prophets’ calls for justice, or the epistles’ statements of equality in Christ) as more authoritative and central, and downplaying others. This is done implicitly by sermon choices and explicitly by some theologians. For example, a liberal preacher might never preach on the herem in Samuel or the rape laws in Deuteronomy, focusing instead on texts that accord with the gospel message as they see it. If pressed, they might say those troubling texts are “not the Word of God for us today.” This resembles what the early church heretic Marcion did (he rejected the OT and parts of the NT), except modern liberals don’t usually remove them from the Bible; they just interpret them in such a way that they carry a different message (or no binding message). The extreme form is to say outright: the God and Father of Jesus Christ is not accurately represented by the genocide-commanding Yahweh in those passages. That veers toward a dualistic view of Scripture. Few mainstream churches go that far formally, but many individual Believers have internal discomfort that leads them to quietly set those texts aside as “not like the God I know.”

·       Emphasizing Metaphor and Myth: Another liberal move is to classify some of these problematic stories as mythic or symbolic, not meant to be taken as literal history or literal commands. For example, the Genesis flood or the conquest of Jericho might be seen not as exact accounts but as legendary stories common to that era, which carry theological symbolism (judgment and new beginnings, or God’s faithfulness) rather than precise moral instruction. If they are not literal, then the moral issue of God actually doing or ordering those things is somewhat softened (though the problem of it being in Scripture remains). Similarly, some read the binding of Isaac as a story that ultimately taught Israel that God does not desire child sacrifice (since God stopped it), thus flipping its implication to a positive lesson – albeit taught via a disturbing scenario.

·       Development of Doctrine and Ethics: Liberal Believers often embrace that Believer ethics have developed under the guidance of the Holy Spirit, beyond the letter of Scripture. They point out that even within the Bible, later texts modify earlier ones – for instance, Israel’s prophets critiqued excessive violence and oppression, moving the dial forward (the prophets “vigorously challenged every kind of injustice and violence” in their context ). This developmental view says the trajectory continued after the Bible too: the abolition of slavery, elevation of women’s rights, rejection of religious coercion, etc., are seen as fruits of the gospel working in history, even if the Bible itself hadn’t fully fleshed them out. Therefore, a modern Believer can in good conscience say “slavery is wrong” or “religious tolerance is good” despite the Bible’s silence or contrary laws, because they see themselves as carrying forward the trajectory initiated in Scripture. Love becomes the supreme hermeneutic; as 19th-century abolitionist and revivalist John Wesley said, “whatever in Scripture is contrary to love is, in that respect, not obligatory.” This captures the liberal ethos: the spirit of the law (love, justice, compassion) always trumps the letter when they conflict.

 

It’s worth noting that not only liberals, but many moderate, everyday Believers who might not label themselves “liberal” often instinctively use some of these approaches. For example, plenty of Believers in pews today simply ignore the harsh Old Testament laws and focus on Jesus. They may not articulate a formal doctrine of errancy, but practically they prioritize. This pragmatic selectivity is a form of the “canon within canon” approach borne of moral intuition. In academia, liberal biblical scholars like those in the “Texts of Terror” tradition (Phyllis Trible, etc.) explicitly shine a light on the Bible’s problematic passages from a feminist or liberationist perspective, sometimes lamenting them rather than defending them, but still finding value in struggling with them.

 

To illustrate a liberal Believer stance: A progressive Believer author might write, “Yes, the Bible includes horrendous things: genocide, slavery, misogyny. Those reflect the cultures in which the Bible was written, not the eternal heart of God. The true Word of God is seen in Jesus—loving, forgiving, non-violent. So we must ‘filter’ the Bible through Jesus. If a scripture passage doesn’t look like Jesus, we question it.” This kind of reasoning is increasingly common in many mainline Protestant circles and among emerging church evangelicals. It allows believers to remain Believer and uphold the central message of the Bible (as they see it) while frankly acknowledging that not everything in the Bible is morally acceptable.

 

One might say modern Believers often perform an internal “wrestling with the text,” similar to how ancient Jewish rabbis wrestled with their scriptures (the Talmud, for example, creatively reinterpreted the rebellious son law until it was impossible to carry out , effectively nullifying it). Today’s Believer interpreters do something analogous: they wrestle and rethink, motivated by the conviction that God ultimately is good and just, therefore scriptures that seem to say otherwise must be handled in a way that upholds that goodness—either by contextual explanation (traditional) or by honestly admitting those verses do not fully capture God’s will (liberal).

 

Having outlined these defenses, we must note that none of them fully erase the difficulties. They explain or mitigate, but from an outsider’s view (or even a concerned believer’s view), questions remain. Thus, it’s important to consider counterarguments to these rationalizations.

 

Counterarguments to Believer Defenses

 

While the above strategies can be meaningful for believers, critics and skeptics – as well as many ethical believers – have raised strong counterarguments asserting that these defenses are inadequate. Here we present some counterpoints to each major line of defense:

·       On Progressive Revelation and Context: Skeptics argue that an all-good, all-powerful God could have established higher moral standards from the start. If slavery is wrong, why not forbid it outright in the Ten Commandments? If God could tell the Israelites not to eat pork or shellfish, He presumably could have told them not to own people or not to treat women as property. The explanation that people were “not ready” for such revelation strikes some as unconvincing – it makes God seem limited by human culture. Moreover, there are examples of ancient moral codes outside Israel that show relative advances (for instance, some cultures prohibited certain kinds of slavery or granted women more rights in some areas). If human societies could sometimes exceed the Bible’s ethics, why couldn’t God have led Israel to be a beacon of a radically different morality? The progressive revelation idea also risks implying God temporarily approved of evil. Saying God tolerated a lesser evil (slavery, etc.) for a time might make sense up to a point (analogous to divorce being allowed for the “hardness of hearts,” as Jesus said), but outright commands to massacre infants or to stone women caught in adultery – these are active things, not just permitted but directly commanded in Scripture. Can God be exonerated for that by claiming “it was a different stage in revelation”? Some ethicists say no – genocide is genocide, regardless of context; a perfectly good deity would not order it at one stage and then later say “actually, love your enemies” at another. The moral contradiction is too stark to be brushed aside as gradual change. As one theologian (David Bentley Hart) has been paraphrased: if we cannot tell the moral difference between Jesus laying down His life in love and Moses laying waste to a city of infants, then what can we know about God at all? . In other words, calling it “progress” doesn’t resolve the dissonance; it actually highlights it.

·       On the Old vs. New Covenant Distinction: Critics note that even the New Testament doesn’t directly reject slavery or patriarchy. Jesus and the apostles did not lead a social revolution against those institutions. So if one says “that was the Old Testament; the New Testament fixes it,” one must face that the New Testament itself contains endorsements of slavery and of women’s subordination (albeit with a more benevolent tone). For centuries, Believers used the New Testament to justify keeping women silent and slaves obedient. It is only in the very recent past that mainstream Believer thought has largely abandoned those readings. This suggests the improvement came more from humanistic and Enlightenment influence than straight biblical exegesis. The Bible itself, taken as a whole, did not unequivocally set out the modern ideals. Furthermore, if we excuse the Old Testament by saying it’s superseded, we risk a form of Marcionism (discarding the OT God as different). Traditional Believerity actually teaches that the God of the Old and New Testaments is the same and does not change. So it’s a theological conundrum: how to reconcile the merciful Father of Jesus with the Yahweh who ordered banishments. Some say the solution is to see continuity (the OT judgment acts are also part of God’s justice, and the NT still has judgment like Hell). Others, more liberal, effectively do side with a kind of Marcionite tendency, prioritizing the NT ethic and sidelining the OT one. But then one must question in what sense the whole Bible is authoritative.

·       On Hyperbole or Misinterpretation: While certainly some war texts might be exaggerated, not all troubling passages can be waved off as hyperbole. The slaughter of the Canaanites is described in such detail and with theological rationale (to avoid idolatry) that it seems more than just “trash talk.” Even if phrases like “utterly destroy” had a rhetorical flair, the intent was clearly to eliminate those populations from the land. The narrative of Jericho, for instance, is not written like a metaphor – it recounts every living thing being killed except one family. If hyperbole, it’s oddly specific. Moreover, archaeology may suggest such total destruction didn’t happen exactly as told (some cities were not destroyed when Bible says, etc.), which could support the “not literal” view. But that opens another can of worms about the Bible’s historical accuracy. Another misinterpretation argument is when apologists say “that verse doesn’t mean what it looks like.” Take Deuteronomy 22:28-29 (rape victim marrying rapist): some argue the Hebrew word might imply seduction, not rape. However, most scholars and traditional translators do read it as rape (in contrast to the scenario in 22:25-27 which explicitly describes rape of a betrothed woman). The apologetic re-reading often appears forced, seemingly trying to sanitize the text after the fact. Critics accuse such approaches of intellectual dishonesty or excessive spin. They contend that the plain reading of many of these texts is what it appears – ethically problematic – and it’s only our modern sensibilities that make us scramble to reinterpret them. As for the idea that women’s silence verses were interpolations – even if true (and many think it plausible for 1 Cor 14:34-35 ), that just shows that early church leaders held those views strongly enough to insert them. The fact remains that those words are in the canonical Bible and have been read as such for centuries. Claiming “that part isn’t original” can be seen as cherry-picking which verses to accept. It undermines a simplistic view of biblical integrity, which is fine for a liberal approach, but then one must admit the Bible itself is not entirely internally consistent on these issues.

·       On Divine Command and Mysterious Ways: The defense that “God’s ways are higher; He had morally sufficient reasons we might not grasp” is philosophically debated. The counterargument is that this essentially nullifies moral reasoning. If genocide or slavery can be good in one circumstance (just because God supposedly said so), then morality becomes arbitrary. One could justify any atrocity by claiming a divine mandate. This is dangerous, as history shows – people have committed atrocities believing they were obeying God (from medieval Crusaders to modern extremist cults). Critics like philosopher Raymond Bradley have posed a dilemma: if you say God commanding genocide is good, you’ve abandoned the very meaning of goodness (it becomes just “might makes right”). But if you say genocide is actually not good, then you must admit those biblical commands were not truly from a good God. It’s a tough problem. Many feel that basic moral principles (like ‘do not murder innocents’ or ‘do not treat persons as property’) are universal, and even God would not violate them because they stem from God’s own just nature. Therefore, they find it more plausible that the texts reflecting such violations are human misattributions to God, rather than literal expressions of God’s will. In essence, to preserve God’s goodness, one might have to concede the fallibility of those scriptures. This is exactly what liberal Believers do. Conservative apologists who hold both “the text is infallible” and “God is good” end up making arguments that sound, to outsiders, like special pleading. For example, William Lane Craig’s suggestion that killed Canaanite children went to heaven and thus it was an act of mercy is widely criticized as a grotesque rationalization—it implies the Israelites were doing a favor by killing toddlers (an idea morally repugnant to our conscience). It also proves too much: by that logic, killing any young child might be “merciful” to send them to heaven before they reach an age of accountability, which is clearly an unacceptable conclusion. Thus, divine-command defenses often raise more ethical problems than they solve.

·       On Allegorical Interpretation: While allegory can provide spiritual readings, critics note that allegorical interpretation is highly subjective. One can make a violent story mean something peaceful by allegory, but that doesn’t change the fact that for many centuries people read it literally and acted accordingly. Allegory was actually used by some Church Fathers specifically to avoid the plain meaning when it disturbed them. For instance, Origen was troubled by the ethics of the conquest, so he allegorized—acknowledging implicitly that the straightforward meaning was problematic. If the text’s literal sense was edifying, there’d be no need to allegorize it. So allegory is seen as a band-aid that doesn’t address why God would inspire such violent stories in the first place. Additionally, not all passages easily lend themselves to a benign allegory. A psalm blessing baby-killing or a law about marrying a rapist – it’s hard to extract a positive allegory from those without twisting beyond recognition. Allegory also doesn’t carry weight in ethical debates – an enslaved person or an oppressed woman in history would not find much comfort in someone allegorically explaining the verses that justify their oppression. They needed actual repudiation of those verses’ authority, which only came through changing how the Bible was applied.

·       On Biblical Trajectory and “Sowing Seeds”: It’s true that Christianity (and Judaism) gradually moved away from some biblical practices (like slavery and polygamy). But historical credit is debatable. For every abolitionist who quoted “all one in Christ,” there was a slave-owner quoting “slaves, obey your masters.” The Bible was used on both sides. Critics argue that the abolition of slavery and advancement of human rights happened in spite of Scripture’s plain sanction of those practices, not because of it. It required either reinterpreting or effectively overruling the letter of Scripture in favor of broader principles. This was not a smooth, inevitable progression from the pages of the Bible alone; it involved human moral development, often influenced by philosophy, empathy, and secular thought. For instance, the Quakers led abolition due to a deep conviction of equality that, while they grounded it in the spirit of Christianity, certainly went beyond anything explicitly spelled out in the Bible. If the Bible really “undermined oppression of women at every turn” as some claim , one wonders why nearly two millennia passed before any church ordained women or affirmed equal rights – perhaps the text wasn’t so clear after all. Similarly, one might point out that Jesus and the early church did not mount a social campaign to end Roman slavery – something modern Believers would certainly do if they were in that context with their current values. Thus, the idea that the Bible set the seed that automatically grew is too generous; human moral conscience and struggle were needed to push beyond the Bible’s confines. Moreover, some seeds in the Bible can produce bad fruit too: the same Bible that inspired Martin Luther King Jr. also was used to oppose interracial marriage (on grounds of biblical separations of peoples). So, appealing to the “overall message” can become a matter of selective emphasis. Critics accuse Believers of cherry-picking the nice parts as “core message” and dismissing the rest as “cultural,” which is precisely what skeptics do in rejecting the Bible. The difference is that skeptics then question the Bible’s authority entirely, whereas Believers want to salvage it by reinterpretation.

·       Practical Harm of Problematic Passages: A strong counterargument is simply pointing to history: those troubling scriptures, when taken at face value, have caused real harm. The Crusades and religious wars were justified in part by Old Testament warfare imagery. The Inquisition and execution of heretics were justified by Old Testament examples of purging evil and NT parables about slaying the wicked. The subjugation of women had an enormous foundation in the explicit words of Paul about women’s roles. Slavery in the New World was vigorously defended with many Bible verses, against abolitionists who had to appeal to broader themes. Children have been abused under the banner of “sparing the rod spoils the child.” The fact that mainstream Christianity today condemns those things is progress, but it required overcoming or reinterpreting those very scriptures. The question a skeptic might pose is: If the Bible is God’s main revelation, why did it not clearly teach those more enlightened morals to begin with, sparing us so much pain? Why did abolition and human rights have to wait until fairly recently and largely in secular contexts to be articulated clearly? This calls into question the claim that the Bible is an ideal moral guide. At best, one might say the Bible is mixed – it has lofty moral ideals (love, justice) and regressive allowances. A humanist observer notes exactly that: “the book was written solely by humans in an unenlightened era, hence it contains many errors and harmful teachings” . From that perspective, the Believer defenses look like rationalizations to avoid this simpler explanation.

·       Internal Biblical Critique?: One might also note that within the Bible, some of these issues are already being critiqued. For example, the prophets (like Amos, Isaiah, etc.) denounce oppression of the poor, exploitation, and excessive violence, moving Israel’s ethics forward . Jesus famously said “Moses allowed divorce because of your hardness of heart, but from the beginning it was not so” (Mark 10:5) – implying that even Mosaic law had concessions that weren’t God’s perfect will. This could be extended: perhaps Moses allowed slavery or harsh retaliation laws due to hardness of heart too. The New Testament also softens some edges (e.g. “Let he who is without sin cast the first stone” in John 8, challenging the stoning law for adultery). These hints support the idea that not every earlier command was God’s ideal. However, a critic would say: if even the Bible shows these corrections, then doesn’t that mean parts of the Bible itself were not fully correct (morally speaking) to begin with? So why insist they were all directly from God? It opens the door to seeing scripture as a dialogical human-divine process, not a monolithic dictation of God’s ethics. Many liberal Believers accept that; conservative ones resist it. But if one resists it, one is stuck defending the morality of genocide or patriarchy on God’s behalf, which as we’ve seen is exceedingly problematic.

 

In light of these counterarguments, some Believers have adjusted their view of biblical inspiration. They maintain faith in God and in Jesus’s teachings, but they view the Bible more as a record of people’s journey with God, containing profound wisdom but also reflecting human limitations. From this vantage, one can frankly say: Yes, the Bible has morally troubling parts. They teach us how far we’ve come and how God can work even through flawed people, but we are not bound to accept those actions as right. For instance, a Believer might argue that Jesus himself provides the counterargument to the OT violence: when James and John wanted to call down fire on a Samaritan village (imitating Elijah’s OT example), Jesus rebuked them (Luke 9:54-56). The lesson? The disciples assumed biblical precedent made that okay, but Jesus revealed a different way – one of mercy. In essence, Jesus overturns his disciples’ violent biblical literalism.

 

From a skeptic’s angle, the ultimate counter is that if a holy book needs such extensive explaining away and selective reading to align with basic moral ideals, perhaps it’s not wholly divinely authored or at least not wholly reliable as a moral authority. The skeptic would say, as Dan Savage did, “We ignore what the Bible says about slavery because the Bible got slavery wrong.” If it got that wrong, maybe it got other things wrong, which means we must use our own moral reason and conscience (informed by empathy, evidence, perhaps the better parts of scripture) to discern right and wrong, rather than treating every biblical command as equal.

 

Many Believers, uncomfortable with that conclusion, have tried to keep the Bible as a foundation while not endorsing its worst parts. This leads to the various harmonizations we described. But each harmonization invites pushback, as shown above. The debate between Believer apologists and critics on these matters is ongoing and often heated. However, in modern times even devout scholars acknowledge these are serious difficulties. As Peter Enns (a Believer biblical scholar) writes, “If God’s Word contains such laws [as herem warfare], what does this say about God? This is a theological dilemma that simply can’t be swept under the rug” – standard answers often “ring hollow” . This candor represents a shift: rather than confidently claiming easy answers, many faithful readers admit the tension and wrestle with it.

 

Ultimately, the conversation about problematic biblical content forces Believers to reflect on the nature of Scripture and revelation. Some move toward a more nuanced doctrine of inspiration (not everything in the Bible directly reflects God’s character; one must interpret through Christ). Others double down on traditional views but emphasize mystery and trust in God’s wisdom beyond ours. On the receiving end, non-Believers often remain unconvinced by the justifications, seeing them as case of special pleading to preserve belief in an ancient text. They point out that if any other ancient book contained these things, people would have no trouble labeling them immoral.

 

In conclusion, addressing the Bible’s problematic content requires careful navigation. Believers who wish to uphold the goodness of their faith have developed thoughtful approaches to interpret these passages in context, to find redeeming aspects, or to subordinate them to the higher revelation of love. Critics, however, challenge Believers to apply the same moral consistency to the Bible that they would apply elsewhere, and not exempt it from critique. They remind that real human harm has been justified by these scriptures, so one can’t just ignore them; one must confront and if necessary repudiate them.

 

Both the formal scholarly analysis and the personal struggle of faith come into play here. From a scholarly perspective, one can trace how attitudes within the Bible and in subsequent interpretation have shifted. From a faith perspective, one must determine how to regard a sacred text that is mixed in its moral content. Increasingly, many Believers find a middle ground: affirming the Bible’s spiritual importance but also acknowledging its imperfections due to the human element. They argue this need not destroy faith; rather, it leads to a more mature faith that isn’t shaken by the fact that Scripture reflects a journey and that we are meant to continue progressing in understanding God’s will (which they center in principles of love taught by Jesus). They also emphasize honesty and humility in reading the Bible, rather than denial or whitewashing. As the Catholic apologist Joe Heschmeyer notes, we shouldn’t be smug or dismissive when these hard questions arise, but engage them with “gentleness and reverence” , recognizing the concern is valid.

 

Conclusion

 

The Bible’s problematic passages – involving slavery, violence, subjugation of women, harsh punishments, and more – present a complex challenge. On the surface, many of these seem to violate the very values of human dignity and compassion that countless people (including Believers) hold dear. A broad summary finds that the Old Testament contains laws and commands which today we deem human rights violations (slavery, genocide, death for minor offenses), and the New Testament, while advancing the ethic of love, still reflects ancient social norms (like patriarchy and acceptance of slavery) that conflict with modern universal values. Some of these passages are merely descriptive of human failings, serving as cautionary tales; others are prescriptive or divinely sanctioned within the text, thus more troublesome.

 

Believers over the centuries have not been blind to these issues. They have interpreted and reinterpreted the troublesome texts in various ways to reconcile them with belief in a just and loving God. Traditional interpretations often argue that these commands had a context – they were steps in a progressive revelation, or special cases of divine judgment, or meant to be read allegorically – thereby trying to defend the overall goodness of the Bible’s message. More modern approaches are willing to critique the Bible from within Christianity, choosing to focus on the core revelation of love and justice and treating those disturbing texts as remnants of a less enlightened time. They may concede that those parts of scripture do not carry normative weight for today and were culturally conditioned.

 

For each defense, there are robust counterarguments asserting that such justifications fall short. The stark reality of what the text says, and the historical harm done under its authority, cannot be fully erased by contextual explanations. Critics maintain that we should be willing to say the Bible contains some morally problematic teachings and that ethical truth must be discerned even apart from or beyond certain scriptures. Indeed, many faithful individuals, while revering the Bible, ultimately follow the spirit of compassion and justice when it conflicts with the letter of a verse.

 

This tension has led to a refinement in many Believer circles of how the Bible is understood: not as a flat code of directives where every line is equal, but as a record that culminates in the revelation of God in Christ, who is the true Word of God (John 1:1). By prioritizing the person and teaching of Jesus, who did not endorse violence or slavery and who elevated the marginalized, Believers find a way to affirm scripture’s overall trajectory while firmly rejecting the problematic elements as incompatible with the character of God revealed in Christ. Traditionalists and liberals may differ in method, but often their end conclusion converges: we do not practice slavery because, notwithstanding its biblical regulation, the gospel of Christ leads us to a different vision; we do not stone adulterers because Christ taught mercy; we do not keep women silent because in Christ there is no male or female – and so on.

 

Thus, even as the Bible remains foundational, its interpretation is not static – it has always involved moral reasoning and doctrinal development. The process of confronting these troublesome passages has itself driven deeper theological reflection and growth. It forces believers to ask, “What is the heart of our faith? What is God truly like?” If the answer is, as the New Testament insists, “God is love”, then everything else in scripture must align to that, or be understood as a partial insight awaiting fulfillment.

 

For non-believers or secular analysts, the conclusion might simply be that the Bible, like any ancient text, contains both good and bad, and we should treat it not as an infallible rulebook but as a historical document – learning from its wisdom while unequivocally rejecting its immoral parts. From that perspective, the best “defense” against the harmful effects of certain scriptures is to frankly acknowledge their wrongness and ensure they are not used to justify injustice today.

 

In any case, the conversation itself – between the text, faith tradition, reason, and moral experience – is valuable. It exemplifies how religious communities evolve ethically. As society continues to uphold universal human rights, Believers largely have joined in affirming those rights, often finding that the core values of their faith (love, the equal worth of all before God) support the same conclusions, even if parts of their scripture do not. Debating the Bible’s problematic content has, in effect, helped sharpen and clarify what Christianity stands for in a modern context: not the letter of ancient laws, but the spirit of love embodied in Christ.

 

This formal analysis has cited scholarly and theological sources to demonstrate these points. In the next section, a more casual, conversational explanation will be provided, covering the same themes in accessible language for a general audience.

Talking about Tough Bible Passages (Casual Explanation)

 

Introduction: The Bible’s Baggage

 

Let’s be honest: the Bible has some pretty troubling passages. People often think of the Bible as a guide to goodness, yet when you actually read it, you’ll encounter verses that make you go, “Wait, that’s in the Bible?!” For example, there are laws in the Old Testament that allow slavery , or that command the death penalty for things like working on the Sabbath or worshipping the “wrong” god . There are stories of mass violence – God commanding the Israelites to wipe out entire nations, including women and children. There are verses that treat women as second-class, telling them to submit and keep silent. And there are even instructions about killing disobedient kids (seriously, look up Deuteronomy 21:18-21 sometime). All of this can clash pretty hard with what we today consider basic human rights and values: equality, freedom of religion, non-violence, protecting children, etc.

 

So, what do we do with all that? How do people who believe the Bible is holy or inspired reconcile these “problematic” bits with their faith in a loving, just God? That’s the big question we’re tackling here. We’ll start by laying out the main types of problematic content in the Bible – things like slavery, violence, the treatment of women, harsh punishments on kids and others. I’ll give examples so it’s clear what we’re talking about. Then we’ll consider whether the Bible itself sees those things as bad or not – sometimes the Bible stories themselves offer a moral lesson, but other times the troubling practice is just taken for granted or even endorsed. After that, we’ll look at how Believers defend or explain these passages. There’s a range of approaches: from very traditional arguments (like “it was a different time” or “God had a plan”) to more modern re-thinkings (like “those verses don’t reflect God’s real heart, which is seen in Jesus”). Finally, we’ll go through some counterarguments – basically, points that skeptics or critics raise to challenge those Believer explanations.

 

The goal is to be fair and clear. This isn’t about bashing the Bible or, on the flip side, ignoring its flaws. It’s about understanding the issues and seeing how people have tried to deal with them. By the end, we should have a good sense of where the tension lies and what kind of answers (if any) can resolve it.

 

Alright, let’s dive into the sticky content first.

 

What Are the Problem Areas?

 

1. Slavery in the Bible:

One of the most glaring issues is that the Bible – both Old and New Testaments – seems to accept slavery as a normal thing. In the Old Testament Law, there are rules outlining how to buy and treat slaves rather than forbidding slavery. For example, Leviticus 25 tells the Israelites they can purchase slaves from neighboring nations and keep them as property permanently (those slaves and their children become the owner’s property) . Hebrew slaves (fellow Israelites) were to be released after 6 years, but non-Hebrew slaves could be kept for life. The law in Exodus even says if a master beats a slave and the slave dies immediately, the master is punished; but if the slave lingers a day or two before dying, no punishment, because “the slave is his money/property” . That’s pretty horrifying to modern eyes – it essentially condones beating a slave as long as you don’t kill them outright.

 

In the New Testament, while there’s a more brotherly attitude, it still doesn’t condemn slavery. Paul, in multiple letters, tells slaves to “obey your earthly masters in everything” and to serve them sincerely . Now, Paul also tells masters to be kind to slaves and mentions that in Christ, slave and free are equal spiritually – but he never says “free your slaves” explicitly. In fact, the letter to Philemon in the New Testament is literally Paul sending an escaped slave (Onesimus) back to his master Philemon. Paul politely asks Philemon to treat Onesimus well and hints that maybe Philemon might voluntarily set him free out of Believer love . But again, no outright command to abolish the institution of slavery. Historically, this was used to justify slavery – slave owners would say, “Look, the Bible doesn’t outlaw it; it even tells slaves to submit.” Abolitionists did find inspiration in broader biblical principles (like “love your neighbor” and “we’re all one in Christ”), but they had to argue against the letter of many verses that were pro-slavery. So it’s a big conflict: human rights 101 says owning people is wrong, but parts of the Bible treat it as just the way it is .

 

2. Holy Wars and Violence:

The Old Testament has some extremely violent content, often presented as commanded by God. The prime example is the conquest of Canaan. Books like Joshua and 1 Samuel describe God ordering the Israelites to wipe out entire populations. In Deuteronomy 20:16-17, God tells them regarding certain cities: “Leave alive nothing that breathes. Completely destroy them”. And Joshua carries this out in several cases – at Jericho, the Israelites kill “men and women, young and old” . In 1 Samuel 15:3, God instructs King Saul to attack the Amalekites and “not spare them; put to death men, women, children, and infants, oxen and sheep, camels and donkeys.” It’s basically commanding genocide. There’s also the story of the Flood (God drowning almost all of humanity) and other acts of divine wrath (like the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah – wiping out cities). Even in the New Testament, the Book of Revelation talks about massive violent judgment at the end of times (though that’s more symbolic and future). And Jesus, while mostly preaching love, has a parable where a king says, “Bring my enemies here and slaughter them in front of me” (Luke 19:27) – which is often noted by critics, though it’s part of a story, not a direct command to his disciples.

 

The problem is clear: these scriptures don’t just depict violence; they endorse it as God’s will in those situations. Today, we consider things like genocide or executing people for religious differences as heinous crimes. We have concepts of just war that explicitly forbid targeting civilians, etc. Yet here in the Bible, we see infants being killed and it’s supposedly God-sanctioned. It really clashes with modern moral values and even with later biblical values of mercy. It also raises the theological question: would a good and loving God really command that?

 

3. Women’s Rights and Role:

The Bible was written in patriarchal societies, so it has a lot that, by modern standards, treats women unfairly or restrictively. For instance, in the Old Testament, women are often viewed as under the authority of their fathers or husbands. There’s a law in Deuteronomy 22 that if a man rapes a virgin who isn’t engaged, the rapist must pay the father some money and marry the girl (and can never divorce her) – effectively forcing the victim to live with her rapist . This was probably meant to ensure the woman wasn’t left destitute (since sadly no one might marry her after she wasn’t a virgin), but from our perspective, it’s horrifying. Another law says if a bride is found not to be a virgin on her wedding night, she shall be stoned to death (Deut. 22:20-21). Adultery laws typically treated women as the property violated. There’s also an odd ritual in Numbers 5 about a wife suspected of adultery – it’s very humiliating and one-sided (nothing similar for husbands).

 

Polygamy (men having multiple wives) is common in the Old Testament among key figures (Abraham, Jacob, David, Solomon, etc.), and while some stories show it causes family strife, it’s not explicitly condemned in the law. Women in those narratives often have very little agency.

 

In the New Testament, Jesus respected women more than was typical for the time (talking with them openly, having female followers, etc.), but when we get to the Epistles, we see teachings that have troubled many: “Wives, submit to your husbands” (Ephesians 5:22) and “I do not permit a woman to teach or to have authority over a man; she must be silent” (1 Timothy 2:12). Paul in 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 says “women should remain silent in the churches…if they want to inquire about something, they should ask their husbands at home”. These verses were used for ages to keep women out of leadership in church and even to justify societal subordination. They directly conflict with the idea that women should have equal opportunities and voices.

 

Even outside the specific commands, some narratives are disturbing from a feminist perspective. In the Book of Judges, there are a couple of gruesome stories: one where a woman (the concubine of a Levite) is raped to death by a mob (Judges 19), and another where a leader (Jephthah) makes a vow and ends up sacrificing his daughter (Judges 11). The text doesn’t explicitly say “and this was bad” – it kind of lets the horror speak for itself, but it doesn’t offer justice or condemnation within the story, which can be uncomfortable.

 

4. Children and Punishment:

We’ve touched on some, but to gather here: The Bible sometimes advocates extremely harsh punishment of children. Exhibit A: “If someone has a stubborn and rebellious son who won’t obey…then all the men of the town are to stone him to death” (Deut. 21:18-21). There’s a long tradition in Judaism that this law was never actually carried out (the rabbis basically said “this never happened and never will” to make it moot) . But the fact that it’s in the scripture is scary – it implies a death penalty for a teenager who’s a persistent glutton and drunkard who doesn’t listen to parents. Today, executing minors – or anyone for non-violent disobedience – is utterly unacceptable.

 

Then there’s the famous line from Proverbs: “He who spares the rod hates his son, but he who loves him disciplines him diligently” (Prov. 13:24) . This and similar verses (Prov. 22:15, 23:13-14) strongly endorse corporal punishment of children – basically, spank/whip your kids to keep them in line, otherwise you’re spoiling them. This has influenced child-rearing in many religious communities, sometimes to a very harmful extent (some extremist parenting books have taken it to abusive levels, quoting these verses). Modern child psychology generally finds that physical punishment can be detrimental, and certainly we’d consider using a “rod” or belt on a child as potentially abusive. So there’s a conflict with today’s emphasis on children’s rights and gentle parenting.

 

And we’ve already mentioned how in war narratives, children aren’t spared (that’s a huge emotional hurdle – imagining infants being killed on God’s command). Or in Exodus, the Egyptian firstborn children die in the final plague – understandable in the narrative as judgment on Egypt, but it’s basically God killing kids to make a point to Pharaoh. So yeah, the Bible has some grim stuff involving children that doesn’t align with “protect the innocent.”

 

5. Freedom of Religion (or lack thereof):

Another area: the Bible, especially the Old Testament, is not big on religious freedom. The first commandment is that you shall have no other gods. Ancient Israel’s law prescribed death for idolatry, false prophecy, or blasphemy. If an Israelite tried to lead you to another god, the law said don’t spare them or even conceal it; you must kill them . In Deuteronomy 13, it even says if your own family member secretly entices you to worship other gods, “your hand shall be first against them to put them to death… stone them to death” . And if an entire town goes after other gods, the Israelites are commanded to destroy the town completely, killing all inhabitants and even livestock, and burn all the spoil (Deut. 13:12-18). That’s basically religious genocide or at least mass persecution. There’s zero concept of “everyone can choose their faith.”

 

Today, freedom of conscience and religion is considered a fundamental human right. We would view executing someone for changing religion or for preaching a different faith as a gross violation. But in the biblical context, Israel was a covenant community where worshipping other gods was seen as treason against the divine king. So it was a theocracy with capital punishment for worship offenses. The New Testament doesn’t set up a civil law like that (Believers didn’t have a nation-state initially), but throughout history, some Believers imported those ideas – leading to things like the persecution of heretics or witches, forced conversions, etc., justified by Old Testament precedent.

 

Summing up these categories: slavery, violence (including war and capital punishment for minor things), sexism, child endangerment, and religious intolerance – these are all found in the Bible. Not every page, of course – the Bible also has lots of positive, loving stuff. But these dark parts stand out sharply against what we now consider ethical. They can feel like “human rights violations in a holy book.”

 

So one might wonder: Did the Bible include these to show examples of what not to do, or did it actually condone them? The answer varies passage by passage. Let’s briefly address that before moving on: sometimes the Bible itself kinda critiques an action (like David’s adultery/murder is clearly shown as wrong and punished), but often it doesn’t voice any criticism – it just states it or even commands it (like the slavery laws). That distinction matters in how we interpret them.

 

Negative Examples vs. Divine Endorsements: What Does the Bible Itself Say?

 

When reading these troubling texts, a key question is: Is the Bible presenting this behavior as acceptable/commanded, or as something bad that happens to teach a lesson? The answer is not one-size-fits-all; different texts function differently.

·       Cases presented as negative (bad examples): Some stories clearly show an act as evil or foolish, even if it’s not explicitly spelled out. For example, the story of the Levite’s concubine in Judges 19 – the horrific gang rape and murder – is part of a larger narrative showing how lawless and terrible things got in Israel when “there was no king…everyone did what was right in their own eyes” . It’s a tale of warning, not endorsement. Similarly, King David’s actions in taking Bathsheba and arranging her husband’s death (2 Samuel 11) are directly condemned by the prophet Nathan, and David faces consequences (2 Samuel 12). So, those are clearly negative examples: the Bible is saying “David sinned” there. Another example: Solomon’s polygamy and idol-worship in his later days is portrayed as his downfall – having many wives who led him to other gods is criticized (1 Kings 11). So even though polygamy was allowed, that narrative suggests it wasn’t great for Solomon’s faithfulness. And the general drift of the Bible’s story of Israel is that violence and disobedience often lead to disaster – e.g., there’s a cycle in Judges: Israel falls into moral chaos (often involving ugly treatment of women and internal violence), and that’s seen as a problem that needs fixing.

·       Cases accepted or endorsed: On the flip side, a lot of the problematic stuff is in the Bible without critique – either neutrally or positively. The prime examples: the laws I mentioned (like those permitting slavery or demanding execution for idolaters). The texts present those as God-given law. There’s no sense in Deuteronomy that “one day this will be seen as harsh”; it’s written as what God commands Israel to do to stay holy. Likewise, when God tells Saul to wipe out the Amalekites, the narrative’s only critique is that Saul didn’t completely do it – he spared the king and some animals, and the prophet Samuel rebukes him for not fully carrying out the ban (1 Samuel 15). So the text’s viewpoint is that Saul should have been more thorough in killing, not that the killing was wrong. That means within the story’s moral universe, that violence was considered right (as a special act of judgment).

 

Slavery is never explicitly condemned in the Bible’s storyline. It’s regulated. Paul even returns a runaway slave, as noted. So the biblical writers did not appear to view the institution of slavery itself as a moral outrage (some of them gave it a new spin, like saying “treat slaves kindly because they are your brothers in Christ,” but that still falls short of condemning the practice).

 

The subordination of women is kinda similar: it’s mostly taken for granted. There are positive female figures (Deborah was a judge and leader, which is interesting; Mary Magdalene was first to announce Jesus’ resurrection). But the rules given, especially in the epistles, reinforce a patriarchal order – and they’re presented as the proper order. There’s no internal biblical critique of “wives submit” – that was upheld as the ideal at the time, with the rationale often being tied to either the order of creation or maintaining good public witness.

 

When it comes to children: the proverb about the rod is literally advice from the Bible (in wisdom literature), not a cautionary tale. The rebellious son law is part of God’s law in Deuteronomy; the text doesn’t say “this never happened” – that interpretation came from later Jewish scholars trying to soften it.

 

In summary: Some violent or sexist narratives show the brokenness of humanity (and thus indirectly teach “this is not what God ultimately wants”). But many problematic verses – especially laws and commands – are given straight, with divine authority, and no hint in scripture that they are wrong or temporary. That’s why these issues are so thorny: we can’t just say, “Oh, the Bible includes bad stuff as an example of what not to do.” Sometimes that’s true, but other times the Bible is actually telling people to do that bad stuff (by our standards).

 

Now, Believers throughout history have been confronted with these texts. And believers who hold the Bible in high regard have come up with various ways to justify, explain, or reinterpret them so that they don’t think God is unjust or that the Bible is immoral. Let’s explore some of those common defenses or rationalizations:

 

How Believers Defend or Rationalize These Passages

 

Believers, being aware of these issues, have not taken them lightly. Over centuries, theologians and preachers developed arguments to defend the goodness of God and scripture despite these passages. Here are some of the most common approaches:

 

1. “It was a different time/culture (Progressive Revelation)” – This is a big one. The idea is that God met people where they were at in a barbaric ancient world and gradually moved them forward. So, certain things (like slavery or patriarchy) were deeply ingrained in the culture; rather than abolish them overnight, God gave laws to regulate and soften them, with the intention of guiding people to a higher ethic over time . For example, they’ll say the Old Testament laws on slavery ensured slaves had some rights (like rest on Sabbath, release of Hebrew slaves in the 7th year, etc.) – which was an improvement on other nations – and that planted seeds for eventually seeing slavery as wrong. Or that God didn’t outright ban polygamy or divorce in the Old Testament, but by the New Testament, the ideal of monogamous marriage was clear (Jesus says divorce was allowed because of people’s hard hearts, but originally it wasn’t so ). So, the argument is: God was patient and dealt with people in stages. In other words, the Bible reflects a moral progression – early on, more primitive practices are allowed, later on, higher standards are revealed. This way, a Believer can say, “Yes, the Old Testament had those things, but that’s not the end of the story – God was leading humanity away from them gradually.” It’s like how you might handle a toddler vs. a teen – you have different expectations. Some Believer apologists explicitly compare early biblical times to spiritual childhood where rules were more basic and strict, and the fullness of moral truth came with Jesus (spiritual adulthood).

 

2. “God had a specific purpose – it’s not meant to be repeated” – In terms of the genocidal commands (like wiping out the Canaanites), defenders often argue that was a unique, one-time command in history for a special purpose (establishing Israel and judging those particular nations for extreme wickedness ). They point out biblical reasons: e.g., in Genesis 15:16, God tells Abraham that the Amorites’ sin is not yet complete – implying God waited hundreds of years until it was “just” to judge them via Israel. So they frame it as divine judgment on really evil cultures (who did things like child sacrifice, etc.), using Israel as the instrument. They also emphasize, “this was a limited command, not a blanket license for violence.” So we today are not authorized to do the same; it was a specific historical situation. This explanation tries to preserve God’s justice: the idea is those people deserved judgment and God gave them lots of time to repent (they cite that Israel was enslaved in Egypt for 400 years, perhaps giving the Canaanites time to change; when they didn’t, the hammer came down ). It’s still uncomfortable, but it’s an attempt to say God wasn’t just randomly endorsing slaughter – it was punishment on extreme evil.

 

Relatedly, with harsh laws (like stoning idolaters or rebellious kids), some say these laws underline how serious sin was in that covenant context. Israel was a special holy nation set apart; any idol worship or major rebellion threatened the whole community’s survival and spiritual health. Thus the drastic measures. But – the argument goes – this was specific to Israel’s theocracy; in the New Covenant (Christianity), excommunication (kicking someone out of church) replaces execution.

 

3. “Hyperbole or Not as Bad as It Sounds” – Another defense is to claim we might be misreading some passages too literally. For instance, some evangelical scholars like Paul Copan suggest that phrases like “utterly destroy” could be Ancient Near Eastern hyperbole – like a sports team saying “we annihilated our opponents” . The evidence they use: some of those texts later mention survivors, or not all towns were destroyed. They compare it to other conquest accounts from that era that use exaggeration. So maybe, they say, the Israelites didn’t actually kill every single person; maybe it was more driving them out or defeating their armies. If that’s true, then the moral problem lessens (though commanding hyperbolic “kill ’em all” is still violent rhetoric).

 

Likewise, some apologists re-examine the troubling rape-marriage law: they’ll argue the Hebrew word in Deut. 22:28 might imply seduction rather than forcible rape (the earlier verses clearly talk about rape of an engaged woman as a crime punished by death; the wordings differ). And they emphasize that it actually doesn’t say “the woman must marry him” explicitly – it says the man must marry her (meaning he’s obligated to provide for her) and can never divorce her. Some Jewish and Believer interpreters spin that as protecting the woman’s future – basically forcing the man to take responsibility for what he’s done for the rest of his life, and she’s not left without support . It’s still a deeply problematic scenario, but they try to say it’s not “rewarding the rapist” so much as punishing him by binding him to care for her (since he can’t just use-and-discard; he’s stuck providing for her needs, and the father got a huge fine out of him too). Many today won’t find that satisfying, but that is an argument made.

 

For slavery, defenders often say, “Biblical-era slavery was not race-based or necessarily lifelong chattel slavery like in the Americas. It was more like indentured servitude or debt slavery. People sold themselves to pay off debts and had certain protections.” Hebrew slaves had a release year. Masters were told to treat slaves well. They’ll point out that kidnapping people to sell as slaves was outlawed (Exodus 21:16 – so the slave trade as practiced later was technically biblically illegal). And in the New Testament, they’ll say Paul subtly undermined slavery by calling the slave Onesimus a beloved brother and by saying masters have a Master in heaven (Col. 4:1) – implying equality in God’s eyes . Also, that eventually Believer ethics led many to oppose slavery. So the argument is “The Bible’s slavery isn’t the same as 18th-century slavery; it was more humane relative to the time.” That might be true in part (slavery in ancient times could be brutal too, but it varied), yet from our perspective any ownership of people is bad. Still, this defense tries to soften it: the Bible ameliorated slavery and planted seeds for its demise (we’ll revisit how true that is).

 

4. “God’s Mysterious Higher Ways” – Some Believers, especially more conservative or fundamentalist ones, might essentially say: “We may not fully understand why God did or allowed this, but we trust God is perfectly just and good. So if God ordered those killings, He had a morally sufficient reason – even if it’s hidden from us.” This is more of a theological surrender to mystery. They might argue that as Creator, God has the right to give life and take it. If we believe God is the source of morality, then by definition whatever God commands (in its proper context) is righteous – and the problem lies in our limited human perspective if it looks wrong. For example, in the story of Abraham and Isaac, God tells Abraham to sacrifice his son, which seems appalling – but then God stops him last second. Some interpreters say God never intended the child to die; it was a test of faith. But others note: Abraham didn’t know that. The point here is that sometimes things God does might shock us, but in the grand scheme they have a purpose (in that story, perhaps to make a point against actual child sacrifice, since God ultimately provides a ram and stops Abraham).

 

So this defense leans on faith in God’s goodness even when scripture is uncomfortable. They might quote verses like “My thoughts are not your thoughts,” (Isaiah 55:8) to say God’s morality can transcend human understanding. Or the story in Job, where Job questions God and God basically responds, “Where were you when I made the world? Can you understand everything?” – implying humans shouldn’t assume they know better than God.

 

While this approach preserves a high reverence for God and scripture, it basically asks one to override their own moral intuition, which is tough. It’s also potentially dangerous – because what if someone today feels “led by God” to do violence? We normally would say “No, that’s wrong,” but the divine command defense could be misapplied. However, within the biblical context, defenders say those extreme commands had specific purposes and ceased after that era, so we’re not to imitate them unless God explicitly commanded (which Believers believe He wouldn’t now under the New Covenant).

 

5. “Allegorical or Spiritual Reading” – Historically, when certain passages seemed unworthy of God, some early Believer thinkers (like Origen, in the 3rd century) interpreted them symbolically. For instance, Origen looked at the genocides and said (paraphrasing), “These wars represent the spiritual battle to root out sin; we must ‘kill’ the vices in our soul completely.” By turning physical violence into a metaphor for personal spiritual growth, they avoided having to think God wanted literal bloodshed. This allegorical method was very common in pre-modern interpretation. Similarly, the erotic or patriarchal elements could be allegorized (like the Church as the bride, etc.). This approach isn’t so popular today for those specific issues, but some pastors still prefer to draw a spiritual principle rather than face the literal text. For example, they might preach on the command to destroy the Canaanites as an analogy: “In our lives, we can’t make peace with the ‘sin’ (symbolized by Canaanites); we have to eradicate it fully.” This avoids saying God ever actually ordered killing people – it treats the text as having a deeper meaning beyond the literal.

 

Now, the more modern/liberal Believer approach (which some of the above start inching towards) might be to admit those parts are not reflecting God’s ideal and should be viewed through the lens of Jesus. A lot of Believers today, especially in mainline Protestant or progressive evangelical circles, will say something like: “The Bible is a progressive revelation; by the time of Jesus, we see the fullest picture of God’s will – which is love, mercy, and inclusion. So we interpret the tough OT passages in light of Jesus. If there’s a conflict, Jesus wins.” Essentially, Jesus’ teachings and example (loving enemies, caring for the least, etc.) override or reframe the old laws. They’ll note Jesus himself said things like, “You’ve heard it said an eye for an eye (which is OT law), but I say turn the other cheek.” He, in a way, superseded a previous command with a higher standard of non-violence and forgiveness. That indicates not everything in the law was God’s final word. Jesus even prevented a lawful execution (the woman caught in adultery in John 8 – “let he who is without sin cast the first stone”). That story, though of uncertain textual origin, aligns with Jesus’s character – he often emphasized mercy over strict judgment.

 

So progressive Believers might frankly say, “God didn’t actually enjoy or endorse those harsh things – that was just the culture and people attributing actions to God. The real heart of God is seen in Christ.” Some even say parts of the OT reflect Israel’s misunderstanding of God’s commands. For example, a pastor might claim “When the Israelites wrote that God told them to kill all the Canaanites, perhaps that was their perspective – but Jesus shows us God wouldn’t actually want that.” This is a more radical re-interpretation that not all Believers are comfortable with, because it implies parts of the Bible are wrong if taken at face value. But it’s a view out there, intended to reconcile these moral issues by essentially correcting the record with Jesus’ ethos.

 

On issues of slavery and women, Believers in modern times typically argue that the trajectory of the Bible is towards liberation and equality, even if not fully realized in the text. They point to things like: the creation story that both man and woman are in God’s image (Genesis 1), or Paul saying “there is neither male nor female, slave nor free, for you are all one in Christ” (Galatians 3:28) as the ultimate principle. They’ll say the early church planted seeds by teaching spiritual equality and brotherhood which later made slavery incompatible with Christianity, leading Believers to abolish it (though, historically, that took 18 centuries!). For women, they highlight that Jesus had women disciples, that the first witnesses of the resurrection were women (in a culture where women’s testimony was discounted), and that there were women leaders in the early church (like Phoebe the deacon, Priscilla who taught Apollos, Junia who is mentioned possibly as a female “apostle” in Romans 16) – suggesting the restrictive passages were more about specific context (like dealing with disorder in Corinth or women who were not educated yet) rather than eternal commands to silence women. Many denominations now ordain women and basically sideline those “keep silent” verses by reinterpreting them as not applicable universally.

 

In summary, Believer defenses range from “It made sense back then, but God led us beyond it” to “We’re misreading it; it wasn’t as evil as it sounds” to “God’s ways are beyond us – trust Him” to “Read it spiritually, not literally.” Each of these attempts to relieve the moral tension in a different way.

 

So, do these explanations resolve the problems? For believers who hold to them, it can help. But there are plenty of people – skeptics, and even many devout Believers – who find some of these answers lacking. Let’s go over some counterarguments and critiques of the defenses above.

 

Counterarguments: Why the Defenses Don’t Convince Everyone

 

Despite the many apologetic arguments, critics point out several issues:

·       “Different times” doesn’t excuse everything: Yes, context matters. But if we’re claiming the Bible is God’s revelation, one could expect it to be ahead of its time morally, not just a tiny bit better. For example, wouldn’t a truly righteous God tell people not to own other people? Or not to do collective punishment on children? The counter is, if some other ancient law codes or philosophers (like some Greeks or others) managed to get certain humanitarian ideas, why couldn’t the Bible’s authors? It raises the question: Were those harsh laws actually from God, or just the culture? If just culture, then can we say those parts of the “Word of God” aren’t really God’s ideal? Many conservative Believers fear that line of thinking because it could undermine biblical authority. But it’s a logical question. Also, the “progression” idea implies God changed the rules or God’s stance, whereas many theologians will say God’s character is consistent. If slaughtering infants was wrong, it was always wrong – so either God had a morally sufficient reason (the classic apologetic assertion) or the Bible reflects human ignorance in attributing that to God. It’s hard to square either way: if one says God truly commanded it and it was okay then, but not okay now, it sounds like moral relativism or a changing God. If one says it was never okay and the Israelites were just wrong about God telling them, then not everything in scripture is accurate. Both are challenging for a view of scripture as wholly reliable morally.

·       Justice of the Canaanite genocide or Amalekite wiping-out: Apologists say those people were extremely wicked (child sacrifice, etc.) . Perhaps true, but every infant and child among them was wicked? Couldn’t an all-powerful God find a way to remove the evil without ordering the killing of babies and pregnant women? The usual response – that those children might be “saved” or go to heaven – is not a biblical statement, it’s an extrabiblical guess to ease conscience . It also basically suggests it’s okay to kill kids because they’ll be in heaven, which is a scary line of reasoning. Additionally, punishing kids for their ancestors’ sins violates later biblical principles (Ezekiel 18 says the son won’t die for the father’s sin). Some say those commands were a specific exception in a unique context – but still, it troubles our sense of fairness. It’s essentially collective punishment on a massive scale. For many, this is one of the hardest things in the Bible to swallow. Believer philosophers themselves debate it. Some like Dr. Paul Copan defend it with context and hyperbole arguments, others like Greg Boyd say “God couldn’t have said that; we must read it as them misunderstanding God.” So even among Believers, there’s not a unified voice.

·       Slavery softening vs. outright condemnation: It’s true the Bible often urged kind treatment of slaves, but it never forbids owning slaves. That’s a stark omission if God wanted to communicate the full moral truth. It took until relatively recently (18th-19th century) for Believers widely to say slavery is a sin – and note, they were opposing Believers who quoted the Bible in favor of slavery. The Southern slaveholders in the U.S. literally argued they had scripture on their side, which was technically true – there are many verses telling slaves to submit. Abolitionists had to use broader principles of love and the spirit of the gospel, but they were going against the letter of scripture. Critics will say, “If the Bible is God’s perfect moral guide, why did it not make the abolition case clearly, instead of giving ammo to slave-owners?” It’s a fair question. The Catholic article referenced acknowledges it’s only in the last 150-200 years that Believers turned strongly against slavery – implying prior to that the mainstream reading of the Bible condoned it. So, one could argue that humans had to morally progress beyond the explicit biblical text (guided perhaps by its higher principles, but still beyond the text’s direct instructions). That suggests the Bible alone wasn’t sufficient to establish what we now see as a fundamental moral truth (that slavery is wrong). From a secular view, it was Enlightenment ideas about liberty and human equality that influenced Believers as much as or more than scripture did on this point.

·       Women’s equality vs. biblical patriarchy: Similar story – only in the last century did most churches start embracing women’s full equality. For the majority of church history, they leaned on verses like “women be silent” to bar women from teaching or leadership. Even today, some denominations do (like Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, many conservative Protestants). They believe they’re staying true to scripture. Others think those verses were cultural or not intended to be universal. But again, that’s an interpretation debate. The Bible doesn’t come out and say, “These restrictions are temporary.” That’s something interpreters read into it to reconcile with modern values. A critic could say: “The Bible reflects the patriarchal norms of its authors. If it were truly declaring God’s timeless will, wouldn’t it have included statements that clearly support gender equality – or at least not include blanket statements about women not teaching?” Those statements gave divine backing to sexism for millennia. Even the idea that “husband is head of the wife” – a lot of Believers still hold to that (in a “loving leader” way), but it’s controversial today because it inherently places one gender under the authority of the other. Modern egalitarian Believers say that was a concession to culture by Paul, not a creation mandate. But to be honest, the Bible itself doesn’t plainly say “I, Paul, am conceding to culture here.” That’s an external theory.

·       Corporal punishment of kids: Many Believer parents today have actually moved away from spanking because of psychological evidence, but some still strongly use those Proverbs verses to justify it. The debate gets fierce. Without digressing too far, the existence of those verses has definitely been used to rationalize what others see as abuse. It’s fair to ask: Wouldn’t God know the harm hitting kids can cause? Why then promote “the rod” as a necessity? Possibly because ancient discipline norms were different – but that means the Bible’s guidance might be culturally bound and not ideal for all time. Admitting that is fine for some Believers (who then don’t feel bound to follow those verses literally), but more literalist believers feel they must obey scripture, rods and all. That can lead to outcomes that conflict with modern child protection standards.

·       Religious intolerance: This one is easier for Believers to distance themselves from by saying, “That was Israel’s theocracy; Jesus taught us to spread faith by persuasion, not by sword.” Indeed, the New Testament church didn’t go around executing pagans. However, once Christianity got state power (e.g. post-Constantine), sadly Believers did sometimes adopt similar intolerance – persecuting paganism, later fighting “holy wars,” inquisitions, etc., often citing Old Testament precedence. The fact that those violent passages are there made it easier for Believers in power to say, “Well, God did sanction violence for righteousness before, maybe it’s okay now.” (Think of Crusaders thinking of themselves like Israel’s armies, or Protestant/Catholic governments executing “heretics” like idolaters). In modern times, most Believers totally reject using force to impose religion, which aligns with democracy and human rights. But one has to admit the Bible’s own texts don’t advocate religious freedom – that concept came from Enlightenment thinkers and was gradually adopted by churches (often reluctantly at first).

 

Now, a very important point: Many Believers today do acknowledge these criticisms to some extent and have adjusted how they view the Bible. Not everyone approaches the Bible as a perfectly dictated rulebook where every command is for all time. A common approach is: The Bible is a record of God’s relationship with humanity, progressing to the fuller revelation in Jesus. We have to interpret earlier parts in light of later parts. That often means saying, “We’re not under those old laws; they were for that time.” But critics respond: “If it was wrong then, why would God even command it for that time?”

 

Some Believers answer that by basically saying not everything in the Bible that is described is actually endorsed by the Bible. For example, they’ll say, “The Bible records people owning slaves or polygamy, but it also shows the problems those caused, so it’s implicitly showing it’s not ideal.” This argument holds weight in some narratives (like polygamy often caused family strife in the stories). But it doesn’t solve clear commands from God in law.

 

There’s also the line between descriptive and prescriptive: A lot of the violence is in narratives (descriptive: what happened), whereas laws and commandments are prescriptive (what should be done). People can excuse some descriptive stuff by saying “that’s just history, not an instruction.” But when God says “do X,” that’s prescriptive and harder to dismiss.

 

A number of Believer thinkers have begun to say frankly: We have to admit these parts of Scripture are ethically problematic. For instance, Peter Enns or Greg Boyd or others have argued that the Bible is fully inspired but also fully human, meaning it carries flawed moral understanding in places. They suggest we “pivot to Jesus” – using Jesus’ life and teachings as the lens to decide what in prior scripture was God’s ultimate will and what was God allowing human hardness or just humans misconstruing God. In practice, this might mean saying, “God never actually wanted genocide; the Israelites thought that’s what God wanted. God’s true character, as revealed in Jesus, is enemy-love, not extermination.”

 

Traditionalists worry that this approach makes us judges over Scripture, picking and choosing. But here’s the thing: we already do pick and choose to some extent. Almost no Believer today is advocating reinstating slavery or stoning adulterers or silencing all women. So, in effect, Believers have rejected or reinterpreted these problematic texts in line with modern ethics (which they often claim are derived from deeper Believer principles, though influenced by secular ideas too). They don’t follow those verses literally, and they give reasons (like covenant differences or context) for not doing so.

 

Critics might say, “That’s convenient. You ignore the bad verses and focus on the nice ones.” But many Believers would reply, “We’re following the trajectory God set – the Bible itself leads us away from those things towards love. It’s not ignoring; it’s interpreting with the whole story in mind.”

 

From an outside perspective, one could argue that a lot of the process is essentially moral reasoning external to the Bible being applied to the Bible. That doesn’t invalidate it – it might just be how humans and religion work. Religion evolves as our moral consciousness evolves, often by rereading the old texts in new lights.

 

One could also highlight that not all Believers agree on these issues. For example, some still maintain very traditional gender roles citing scripture, while others champion full equality also citing scripture. On violence, most Believers now are non-violent in practice, but a few might still use just-war theory or even think in certain cases violence is warranted (like self-defense or defense of others – which is a different scenario but related to how strictly they take Jesus’ commands like “turn the other cheek”). On corporal punishment, some Believers champion it citing “spare the rod spoil the child,” others decry it. So interpretation varies widely.

 

To wrap up the counterarguments: they generally say the apologetics often involve special pleading – meaning if these texts were in any other ancient book, we’d condemn them outright. We only try to soften them because they’re in the Bible, which we presume to be holy. For instance, if the Quran or any other scripture had a verse like “happy is he who dashes your infants against the rocks” (which is actually Psalm 137:9, spoken by an Israelite about Babylonian babies out of vengeance), Believers would probably cite it as evidence of that religion’s violence. But since it’s in our own text, we contextualize it as a raw human emotion, not a theology. (And indeed, that psalm doesn’t reflect God’s command, just the speaker’s feelings – but it being in the Bible is still jarring.)

 

Ultimately, whether these defenses are satisfying or not varies from person to person. Believers who trust the goodness of God and scripture often find ways to reconcile or at least live with the tension (seeing it as partly mystery, partly context). Non-believers or ex-believers might conclude that these problematic parts show the Bible is a human product of its time, not a flawless divine moral guide. Some believers even come to something of that same conclusion (that it’s a human product as well as divine inspiration) and adjust their doctrine of scripture accordingly, without abandoning faith.

 

From a practical standpoint in Christianity today, most will unequivocally say: Slavery is wrong, genocide is wrong, child abuse is wrong, misogyny is wrong, religious coercion is wrong. They will back these stances using the overall biblical message of love, the value of each person, and the example of Christ. But they will also (to differing degrees) acknowledge that there are Bible verses that, on the surface, say otherwise. So it comes down to hermeneutics – how to interpret the Bible.

 

Many adopt the principle that Jesus is the ultimate revelation and that he fulfills and even corrects the past. So if there’s a conflict, they side with Jesus. Jesus never condoned slavery (he didn’t explicitly condemn it either in the gospels, but his teachings on how to treat others undermine it), Jesus elevated women in many ways relative to his culture, Jesus didn’t lead wars, and he welcomed children and said to become like children (quite opposite of stoning them!). So, focusing on Jesus gives a very different tone. If one believes Jesus is God’s true character revealed, one can argue God’s true heart was always against those harsh things, but God had to work with humanity incrementally or those parts were human misunderstanding that got included in scripture.

 

This is, admittedly, a more theologically complex solution than simply “every verse is God’s direct command for all time.” But it’s how a lot of thoughtful Believers reconcile faith with ethical progress. It’s like the Bible is seen as containing a trajectory or direction – starting from primitive understanding and moving toward the ethic of love. As one Catholic article put it, “Revelation is suited to the moral level of distant times… the Bible narrates certain things without denouncing their immorality in the way we would today” . That acknowledges, from within faith, that yes, those things were immoral by today’s standards, and we only know that because God has brought us further along (through Christ and through the Spirit-led growth of moral insight).

 

For a skeptic, that might sound like just retrofitting – giving credit to God for what could simply be human social evolution. But for a believer, it’s not just secular progress; it’s God’s guidance over time (often working through human agents, Believer or not, even through secular movements to challenge the church to be more like Christ).

 

To conclude this casual explanation: The Bible does have problematic content, no doubt. Believers have wrestled with it in various ways:

·       Some by deeper study and context (to try and defuse it).

·       Some by emphasizing the difference between Old Covenant and New Covenant (saying Jesus changed things).

·       Some by saying we need to interpret all scripture through the lens of Jesus’ sacrificial love and mercy, which will override those harsh aspects.

·       And some, unfortunately, have in the past tried to use the problematic verses to justify doing wrong (like using the Bible to support slavery or abuse), which most Believers today would strongly reject as a misuse of scripture.

 

There isn’t a single neat answer that satisfies everyone. It often comes down to one’s view of what the Bible is and how God chose to communicate. If one expects the Bible to be a straightforward rulebook with all timeless rules, these passages are extremely hard to reconcile. If one sees the Bible as a record of a journey toward a fuller revelation in Jesus, then one can accept that earlier stages had imperfect ethics.

 

From a faith standpoint, many will say: The core message of the Bible is love – love God, love your neighbor – and that core is what we hold onto as infallible, whereas some peripheral regulations were for a time and place. That may sound like cherry-picking to some, but it’s a way to preserve the central thrust (which even critics often admire – e.g. the ethics of the Sermon on the Mount) while acknowledging not everything in the text aligns with that central thrust. It’s a tension between the letter and the spirit.

 

For someone evaluating the Bible, it’s good to be aware of these issues. The Bible is not just a single voice – it’s a library with various voices and developments. Understanding that can help one approach it more critically and thoughtfully, rather than either blindly accepting everything or throwing it all out due to the bad parts.

 

In the end, a person’s response might range from:

·       Strengthening their faith in a God who patiently worked through flawed people (if they take the faithful view),

·       Or to seriously question how “perfect” the Bible is and adjust their beliefs about scripture,

·       Or even to reject the Bible’s authority entirely on these moral grounds.

 

That’s a personal journey. Here, our aim was to clearly present what the problematic content is, how it’s presented in scripture, how Believers try to defend it, and what counterpoints exist. With that information, one can make a more informed judgment or continue the conversation.

 

It’s certainly one of the most challenging aspects of biblical religion: squaring an ancient text with modern moral understanding. Many would argue this ongoing grappling is a healthy part of a living faith – we don’t just mindlessly quote verses; we have to deeply think about ethics, context, and the heart of the message. In doing so, believers can claim they are actually following the example of Jesus, who told the Pharisees to not neglect the “weightier matters of the law – justice, mercy, and faithfulness” (Matthew 23:23). That implies that some matters (like love and justice) are weightier than rituals or regulations.

 

So, if justice and mercy say “don’t enslave or harm others,” Believers today choose those principles as weightier than the ancient rules regulating slavery or commanding violence. They would say that this is in line with what the Bible as a whole – especially the teachings of Jesus – leads us to. Critics may remain skeptical and view it as the religion catching up to secular morals. Either way, the result is that mainstream Christianity no longer upholds those problematic instructions – and in fact, often leads campaigns for human rights, precisely appealing to Believer values (ironic given the past, but true in many cases today).

 

To sum up: The Bible’s problematic content (slavery, violence, sexism, etc.) is real and must be acknowledged. Within the Bible’s narrative, some of it was simply part of the culture and even commanded for that context. Believers have developed interpretive frameworks to handle these issues, focusing on the overarching message of love and the example of Christ to guide which scriptures reflect God’s ideal and which were temporary or human elements. Counterarguments challenge those frameworks, pointing out inconsistencies and moral issues. It’s an ongoing dialogue – one that has actually driven much positive change in how the Bible is applied. In a way, wrestling with these texts has helped religious communities clarify what their faith is truly about (e.g., deciding that yes, the heart of God’s will is love and justice, so anything that contradicts that, even if in scripture, has to be read in context or left behind).

 

In modern Christianity, you’ll rarely find anyone defending those troubling practices as acceptable now – that itself speaks to how the faith has evolved in understanding. Many believers would say that’s because of the influence of Jesus and the Holy Spirit guiding human conscience. Skeptics might say it’s humanistic progress pressuring religion to reform. Possibly it’s both. But at the end of the day, the majority of Believers today strongly affirm universal human rights and would distance themselves from the problematic content of their own scriptures, often with a nuanced theological explanation.

 

For a reader coming across these issues, it can be jarring, but it also opens up important conversations about interpretation, the nature of scripture, and moral theology. It forces one to think deeply – which, arguably, is better than unthinking acceptance. As one learns, the Bible is not a monolithic code of conduct; it’s a complex collection of stories, laws, poetry, letters – spanning a long period. It reflects growth and conflict. Understanding that context can help make sense of why not everything in it aligns with what we now consider good. And for Believers, it underscores the need to read wisely and compassionately, always considering how to live out the core ethic of love that they believe the Bible (and supremely Jesus) teaches, even if that means setting aside certain prescriptions that belonged to another era.

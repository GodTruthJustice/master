Detailed Analysis of Genesis 3: Why the Bible May Not Reflect God’s True Words or Will

Many people believe the Bible is directly from God or at least inspired by God. But if we take a closer look at Genesis 3, we might start to wonder if this is actually true.

The Story of the Serpent and the Fall

Genesis 3 tells us about a serpent—often thought to represent Satan—who convinces Eve to eat fruit from the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil, despite God’s clear warning not to. Usually, the serpent is described as deceitful, but was he really lying? Let's examine the serpent’s exact words:

Genesis 3:4-5: "You will not certainly die," the serpent said to the woman. "For God knows that when you eat from it, your eyes will be opened, and you'll be like God, knowing good and evil."

Interestingly, a careful reading reveals two points where the serpent spoke truthfully:

1.    Knowledge of Good and Evil: After Adam and Eve ate the fruit, even God admitted:

Genesis 3:22: "And the LORD God said, 'The man has now become like one of us, knowing good and evil.'"

Clearly, the serpent was telling the truth about gaining knowledge.

2.    Not Immediately Dying: Adam and Eve didn’t die right after eating the fruit. Instead, death came later because God kept them from the Tree of Life:

Genesis 3:22-23: "He must not be allowed to reach out his hand and take also from the tree of life and eat, and live forever." So, God expelled them from Eden.

Therefore, the serpent's statement about not necessarily dying was technically correct. Eternal life remained possible through the Tree of Life, but God deliberately withheld it, thus ensuring mortality and suffering.

God's Response: Authoritarian and Cruel?

Rather than showing forgiveness or compassion, God responded very harshly:

·       He expelled Adam and Eve from paradise, making their lives difficult.

·       He brought pain into childbirth, made daily labor hard, and introduced illness and eventual death (Genesis 3:16-19).

These punishments underscore God's primary concern—maintaining his authority and exclusivity of power and knowledge. Rather than exhibiting love, this depiction resembles a tyrannical ruler punishing rebellion severely to protect his status. Similar themes appear in various mythologies and religious texts, illustrating how divine authority often serves to reinforce human power structures.

Religious Implications: God’s Word or Satan’s Influence?

From a religious perspective, Genesis 3 fails to depict a loving and compassionate God. Instead, human suffering appears directly tied to God's punishment for seeking knowledge—not something inherently evil. The portrayal of God as a figure who punishes knowledge and autonomy aligns with authoritarian ideals, not divine love. A God of love would likely encourage enlightenment, not curse it with pain and death. This raises deep questions: Is the God depicted here truly good, or could this figure represent something darker and oppressive? It’s possible the story reflects human authoritarian ideas or even harmful ideologies influenced by darker motives. Hence, it’s possible that the "God" depicted here may not be God at all, but rather Satan. Parts of the Bible wasn’t God’s word but came from humans who were clearly under the corruption of Satan’s will!

Political Implications: A Tool for Authoritarian Control?

Politically, Genesis 3 can be viewed as propaganda devised by authoritarian regimes to reinforce obedience and suppress dissent. The narrative strongly emphasizes absolute submission to authority, framing curiosity and the pursuit of knowledge—core components of human freedom—as inherently sinful and punishable. History provides numerous examples, such as medieval European monarchies and churches, using religious texts like Genesis 3 to justify and strengthen their authoritarian rule and control over populations.

Conclusion: Whose Words Are These?

After analyzing Genesis 3, we see that the Bible—or at least some parts of it—might not reflect the true words of a loving God. Instead, they may show the influence of human authorities or oppressive ideologies which are will of the devil Satan.

Beyond Genesis 3, other parts of the Bible also contain troubling ideas, including support for slavery (Leviticus 25:44-46), acts of violence (Deuteronomy 20:16-17), oppressive attitudes towards women (1 Timothy 2:11-12), and harsh child discipline (Proverbs 23:13-14). Such content aligns more closely with authoritarian thinking rather than genuine divine compassion.
